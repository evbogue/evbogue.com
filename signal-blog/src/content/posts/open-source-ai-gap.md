The standard response from closed AI labs when pressed on open source competition has been consistent: "Open models lag by 12-18 months." It's a comfortable framing. It implies a stable equilibrium where frontier capability remains proprietary long enough to build a business around it.

That framing is no longer defensible.

The latest generation of open weight models benchmarks within a few points of GPT-4-class performance on most standard evaluations. More importantly, the gap on the tasks that actually matter for enterprise deployment — code generation, document analysis, structured data extraction — has effectively closed for a large percentage of real workloads.

## Why This Happened Faster

Two things accelerated the convergence. First, the research producing frontier capabilities stopped being proprietary at the same rate it used to be. Key techniques get published, get reproduced, get improved by the research community faster than any single lab can move. Second, the compute costs required to train competitive models dropped faster than expected, meaning more organizations can afford to train serious models.

The result is that the moat around closed frontier models is narrowing to the very top of the capability distribution — complex reasoning, novel problem-solving, tasks that genuinely require the best available model.

## The Strategic Implication

For enterprises, this is good news. The choice between closed and open models is increasingly a question of preference and workflow rather than capability. For closed labs, it raises an urgent question about where durable competitive advantage actually lives if the models themselves don't stay differentiated.

The honest answer, increasingly, is that it lives in deployment infrastructure, safety tooling, and enterprise relationships — not in the models themselves.
