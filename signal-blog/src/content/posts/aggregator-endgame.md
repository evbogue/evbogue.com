For the past decade, the dominant frame for understanding tech competition was aggregation theory. Whoever controlled the interface between users and suppliers would capture the value. Google aggregated information. Facebook aggregated social graphs. Amazon aggregated commerce. The suppliers — publishers, businesses, sellers — became increasingly dependent on platforms they didn't control.

The frame still works. What's changed is who counts as an aggregator.

## The New Aggregators

OpenAI is now an aggregator. It sits between users and the entire corpus of human knowledge, answering questions directly rather than routing people to sources. The more capable the model, the less often users click through to anywhere. Publishers who spent fifteen years learning to optimize for Google are now watching the same dynamic play out one layer up.

But here's what the original aggregation theory didn't anticipate: the aggregators are eating each other.

Google built an aggregator for documents. OpenAI built one for knowledge. Apple is quietly building one for tasks. They're not competing in adjacent markets anymore. They're competing for the same session — the moment a user has a question or wants something done.

> The question isn't which aggregator wins. It's whether any underlying ecosystem survives to be aggregated.

## What Breaks

Three things are structurally at risk.

**Discovery.** The open web was built on the assumption that search was a routing layer, not a destination. When the routing layer becomes the destination, new voices stop having a path to audience. The Matthew effect accelerates: existing brands get cited by AI, new entrants don't exist until they're large enough to be in training data.

**Incentives for production.** If AI systems can synthesize the answer to most questions from existing content, the marginal return on producing new content falls. Not to zero — someone has to write the thing that gets synthesized — but to a level that can't sustain the infrastructure that produced quality journalism, research, and analysis.

**The feedback loop.** Good epistemics depend on a diversity of sources that can be traced, questioned, and updated. A world where most people get their information from a small number of model providers is a world with a very small number of chokepoints for both error and manipulation.

## Who Survives

The honest answer is: organizations with direct relationships with their audiences. Not mediated by search, not dependent on social referrals, not hoping to be cited by an AI.

That means email lists. It means subscription models. It means building something people come back to directly, because they trust it specifically, not because an algorithm surfaced it.

It's a smaller world than the one the open web promised. But it might be a more sustainable one.
