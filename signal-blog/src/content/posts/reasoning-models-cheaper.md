Six months ago, running a frontier reasoning model on a complex enterprise workflow cost roughly the same as employing a junior analyst for a few hours. Today it costs less than a cup of coffee. The curve didn't bend — it broke.

## What Changed

The efficiency gains from the last two generations of training techniques have compounded in ways that even the labs didn't fully forecast publicly. Smaller models with better reasoning architectures are outperforming larger models from eighteen months ago at a fraction of the inference cost.

The practical implication is that the "it's too expensive to run at scale" objection — which was the single most common reason enterprises gave for not deploying AI beyond pilots — has largely evaporated for everything except the most demanding workloads.

## The Enterprise Tipping Point

Enterprise software adoption typically follows a threshold model. Usage climbs slowly until it crosses some combination of capability, cost, and organizational familiarity, then accelerates sharply. The cost component of that threshold just moved dramatically.

What this means in practice: companies that were running AI in isolated departments are now getting pressure from finance and operations teams to expand deployment across the organization. The bottleneck has shifted from "can we afford this" to "can we manage this."

## What Comes Next

The companies positioned to benefit most aren't necessarily the model providers. Inference commoditizes. The value moves to whoever owns the workflow layer — the systems that route tasks to the right model, maintain context across sessions, and integrate with existing enterprise data.

That's a different competitive landscape than the one being discussed in most coverage of the AI industry right now.
