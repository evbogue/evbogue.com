Substack built its dominance the same way every great platform does: by making the default option so convenient that switching costs accumulate faster than frustrations do. Email list portability, clean UI, built-in payment processing, a reader network that surfaces new writers to potential subscribers — all of it added up to a platform that was genuinely hard to leave even for writers who had reservations about the company's content moderation decisions.

That calculus is shifting.

The convergence of several factors — improved tooling for self-hosted publishing, better email infrastructure options, and a growing number of successful writers who've demonstrated that audiences will follow them off-platform — has created genuine optionality for the first time.

## What Changed Technically

The technical barrier to running a subscription newsletter outside Substack used to be real. Managing email deliverability, building a payment integration, maintaining a readable archive — these required either engineering resources or stitching together enough third-party services that the maintenance overhead was significant.

The new generation of publishing tools has largely solved this. Several platforms now offer Substack-competitive features with different trade-offs on ownership, revenue share, and customization. More importantly, writers who want full control can now get there without building anything themselves.

## What Hasn't Changed

The Substack reader network still matters. The ability to be discovered by readers browsing the platform, to be recommended by other writers with audiences, remains a meaningful acquisition channel that self-hosted publishing can't replicate.

Writers with established audiences can leave. Writers still building them face a harder trade-off.
