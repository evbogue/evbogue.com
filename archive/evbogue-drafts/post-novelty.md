<p><i>Written by Ev Bogue | Follow me on <a href="https://plus.google.com/116298207273891385223/">Google+</a></i></p>
<p>I forget sometimes that it’s been over a decade since the world started blogging. Evan Williams launched Blogger in 1999. </p>
<p>It kind of shocks me when I consider that I’ve been blogging personally and professionally for more than a decade.</p>
<p>Many of the earlier discussions about what blogging is, what it will be, and how it can used are now over. The medium has settled into itself in form. </p>
<p>For example: it’s pretty much assumed that a blog post will have headlines, permalinks, and (if it wants to be taken seriously) well edited text.</p>
<p>I’d like to suggest that we’re at the end of the novelty age of blogging.</p>
<p>What does this mean? It means that the simple act of having a blog is no longer the fundamental decider in whether or not people read your blog. </p>
<p>What matters now is the <strong>practice of your presence</strong> on your blog. </p>
<p>//</p>
<p>The post-novelty age of blogging assumes that everyone knows what a blog is. If they want one, they have one by now. It also assumes that very few people are under the mistaken impression anymore that blogging is a career choice. Blogging is a tool, not a job. It might be part of your goal to use the tool, and then set it down when you’re done with it. </p>
<p>What matters now is refinement — just because we have a blog doesn’t mean anyone wants to read it.</p>
<p>The age of making money introducing people to the tool is over, and the age of teaching people how to refine their use of the tool has begun.</p>
<p>This means we’re the craftsman of the blogosphere now. We’re the architects and curators of a medium that’s where the initial map has already been built. </p>
<p>What is our project now? </p>
<p>To work on our craft.</p>
