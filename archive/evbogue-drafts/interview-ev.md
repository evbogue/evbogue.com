<h4>How to conduct an interview</h4>
<p>Over the last two years I&#8217;ve been interviewed hundreds of times. Here are a few of the things I&#8217;ve learned, and apply when I conduct my own interviews.</p>
<p><strong>1. Familiarize yourself with the subjects&#8217; work.</strong></p>
<p>The road to a bad interview is ignorance. Avoid this by learning as much about the interviewee as possible before approaching them. I&#8217;ll typically read as much as possible about a person before I contact them to ask questions.</p>
<p><strong>2. Come up with an overall interview theme.</strong></p>
<p>An interview needs to focus on one specific topic. Many questions I receive seem to have little or no connection with each other. Avoid this by picking an overarching theme for the interview.</p>
<p><strong>3. Craft a few specific questions.</strong></p>
<p>Interviews don&#8217;t need to have a million questions. A few good questions are all a subject needs.</p>
<h4>Questions for your questions:</h4>
<p>The next step in preparing an interview is asking questions of your questions. Here are typical questions I&#8217;ll ask before sending an interview.</p>
<p><strong>1. Is this question relevant to the subjects current work?</strong></p>
<p>No one likes getting asked about work they were doing five years ago. Ask about what they&#8217;re working on now.</p>
<p><strong>2. Have I already decided/assumed what the answer will be?</strong></p>
<p>If your cup is full, it leaves little room for the interviewee. Leave room in your question for an answer.</p>
<h4>The ask</h4>
<p>Finally, when you&#8217;re asking for an interview with a subject, it&#8217;s important to respect their time and focus. Here are a few things to give up-front when requesting an interview. </p>
<p><strong>1. Ask permission to send interview questions.</strong></p>
<p>Many people are incredibly busy, and might not have time to answer interview questions. It&#8217;s important to ask whether it&#8217;s okay first, before sending questions. (You can ignore this for me, because I&#8217;ve asked you to interview me.)</p>
<p><strong>2. State the interview theme upfront. </strong></p>
<p>What is the over-arching theme in the interview? Make this clear, concise, and easily understandable so that the interview can thrive within a container.</p>
<p><strong>3. When do you need the interview by?</strong></p>
<p>Next week, next year? Deadlines are a good way to get things done quickly, so no one forgets about the interview.</p>
<p><strong>4. Let them know when you&#8217;re planning to publish the interview. </strong></p>
<p>Example: Next tuesday, I&#8217;d like to publish this interview. It&#8217;s best to publish interviews quickly after you&#8217;ve interviewed. The world is moving faster, waiting three months can change a lot of things.</p>
<p><strong>5. Who will read the interview?</strong></p>
<p>Do you have an audience? Who are they? How many are there? These are good things to provide to anyone you&#8217;re trying to interview &#8212; especially when you begin moving into interviewing micro-celebrities and other people who&#8217;ve been working hard on their craft.</p>
