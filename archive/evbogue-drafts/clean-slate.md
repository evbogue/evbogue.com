<p><a href="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5292/5430954007_4f9eb7ba62_o.jpg"><img class="alignleft" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5292/5430954007_4f9eb7ba62_o.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="300"/></a></p>
<p>The Internet is evolving faster, becoming multidimensional at an incredible rate.</p>
<p>Where we used to engage on the Internet at a flat level, we&#8217;re now finding ourselves traversing many different stages in the consciousness of our presence out on the Net.</p>
<p>We used to be able to choose one or two ways to engage. You were a blogger who emailed or a writer who commented.</p>
<p>Nothing was ever simple in the web of the past, but at least we could learn the technical knowledge and then maybe you&#8217;d have a shot at successfully punching past the noise.</p>
<p>In many ways, up until this moment many of us were fighting a technical battle. Our work was taking the structures of the Net and then attempt to bend them to our will.</p>
<p>Then suddenly, at the dawn of the second decade of the 21st century, our relationship with technology shifted.</p>
<p>We were no longer creating it, instead, it was creating us.</p>
<p>When your relationship to technology changes in this way, it&#8217;s time to re-evaluate, restructure.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s time for beginner&#8217;s mind.</p>
<p>Over the next few months, I hope you&#8217;ll watch as this space morphs into a brand new virtual world.</p>
<p>Your first 10,000 hours on the Net are all about push. The next, an edit.</p>
