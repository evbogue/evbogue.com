<p><i>Written by Ev Bogue | Follow me on <a href="https://plus.google.com/116298207273891385223/">Google+</a></i></p>
<p>In personal finance, they talk about a mental state called poverty mentality.</p>
<p>This is an incredibly common state of mind for people. I know, because I find myself slipping into it once in awhile. </p>
<p>I know I have poverty mentality when I look at the price of an Americano, and if it’s 50 cents more than the coffee shop that’s a mile away, I hike the mile to save the 50 cents.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, I’ve lost an hour of work trying to keep 50 cents.</p>
<p>The most difficult thing about poverty mentality is it’s also contagious. If I’m hanging out with a group of people who are coupon cutting, chances are I’ll start counting quarters as they go out too.</p>
<p>Last year I read a couple of key books on personal finance in order to wrap my head around how people think about money. </p>
<p>One of the books (I forget which) emphasized that major expenses are really the only ones that we need to worry about. College, a house, a car. These all have price tags anywhere from $10,000 − $500,000. Fancy time machines, hoverboards, and other fancy gizmos probably fall under this range as well — when they start to exist.</p>
<p>What the book said was this: if you’re coupon cutting at the coffee level, you’re never going to save up for a big purchase. All of the mental energy that goes into trying to weigh whether the $2.99 coffee is worth it, never pays off. Saving 50 cents every day, for a year, is only $182.50. If we’re running a successful <a href="/posts/books">Minimalist Business</a>, that’s base two-days of solid work to make up that money. $182.50 is definitely not going to buy us a house.</p>
<p>So, how wise is it for me to coupon cut in order to save for a new hoverboard?<br/>
//</p>
<p>On the hand, there’s a mentality that I’m going to call infinite mentality.</p>
<p>This is the mindset that comes forth when I realize that I’m never going to save up for a hoverboard by using a piggy bank for quarters. Instead, I start to invest all of my energies into manifesting wealth instead of hoarding what I already have.</p>
<p>This is a mentality of inspiration, rather than desperation.</p>
<p>How does this mentality come into existence? That’s the harder question. Much of it has to do with believing that there is money out there, it’s flowing, but I have to figure out how create enough value for it to flow towards me.</p>
<p>It’s easier said than done. It’s a feeling of arriving and knowing.</p>
