<p><i>Written by Ev Bogue | Follow me on <a href="https://plus.google.com/116298207273891385223/">Google+</a></i></p>
<p><a href="http://evbogue.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/L1010721.jpg"><img title="L1010721" src="http://evbogue.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/L1010721.jpg" alt="" width="100%"/></a>I have two notebooks. I purchased them both from <a href="http://paper-hammer.com/">Paper Hammer</a> in Seattle. They’re manufactured by <a href="http://mightytieton.com/tieton-made/marquand_editions_tieton/">Marquand Editions</a> in Tieton, WA. When I buy one of these notebooks I’m supporting an organization called <a href="http://www.mightytieton.com/">Mighty Tieton</a>, which is attempting to reinvigorate the economy of a small town in Washington that’s been hit hard by the recession. They make absolutely gorgeous notebooks, which you can see from the photo I’ve attached.</p>
<p>The first notebook (the light tan colored one) is a sketchbook for ideas. No page is sacred. I tend to write everything down that occurs to me in this notebook.</p>
<p>The second notebook (the one with the blue lines) is for my next book, Untether. I write only things related to Untether in this notebook. By differentiating, I know that any idea that comes into my mind could potentially end up in the my book. In this way, the ideas don’t get lost.</p>
<p>In the past, I used Moleskine notebooks, but when a better option came into existence, I upgraded.</p>
<p>Whenever I need an idea to <a href="/posts/how-i-write">write into Scrivener</a>, I check in with my notebooks. There always plenty of ideas in there.</p>
<p>If I don’t have ideas, and I’ve run out of ideas in my notebooks, then I realize that it’s time to sit and brainstorm. I turn off my computer, go outside, and write in my notebooks until I have plenty of ideas to work with.</p>
