<h4>How to use the Internet to get off the Internet</h4>
<p><a href="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5024/5548185642_7a1fa3f00c.jpg"><img class="alignright" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5024/5548185642_7a1fa3f00c.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="300"/></a></p>
<p>For as long as the Internet has existed, people have wanted to get off it.</p>
<p>As the Internet grows, so does the level of noise that we have to deal with every day. There are an estimated 1,970+ million people clicking around on the planet right now, and all of them probably wish they didn&#8217;t have to check their email every fifteen minutes.</p>
<p>I&#8217;m usually not the kind of person who sets goals in life, but if I could set one goal, I think it&#8217;d be to turn off the Internet forever.</p>
<p>This is challenging, because I make my living over the Internet. Increasingly, many of us do.</p>
<p>Over the last few years, I&#8217;ve tried a number of radical experiments. I quit my job. I lived on less than $3,000 for three months. I reduced my possessions to less than 47 things. I lived in five different American cities. I built a zero-overhead online business that basically runs itself.</p>
<p>However, my favorite experiment to date was probably the most difficult. For the month of August 2010, I took a digital sabbatical.</p>
<p>I turned off comments on my blog, scheduled a few blog posts, and booked a ticket and disappeared into the north woods of Wisconsin. For one month I left my iPhone off. I didn&#8217;t check my email. I didn&#8217;t tweet. I just lived my life as if the Internet didn&#8217;t exist.</p>
<p>When I came back, I discovered that my digital business was more profitable than when I left.</p>
<p>In many ways, taking a break from the Internet made it possible for me to re-focus my priorities, so I could focus on what was important in my work.</p>
<p>What was out? Checking email constantly. Blog comments. &#8220;The conversation&#8221;. In: yoga, living in the world, focusing on the craft.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>In <em><a href="/posts/augmented-humanity">Augmented Humanity</a></em>, I wrote that technology is growing strong enough to allow us to turn off the screens &#8212; if we should choose.</p>
<p>I want to choose. I also want to show you how to choose.</p>
<p>This series will is on how to use the Internet to turn off the Internet.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>Living without the Internet isn&#8217;t easy. When you don&#8217;t have an iPhone to distract you all of the time, you have to take a look at yourself. Do you like what you see?</p>
<p>Distraction isn&#8217;t just keeping busy, it&#8217;s also stopping you from asking hard questions about yourself.</p>
<p>In order to work towards a place of no distraction, you have to peel back the layers of yourself in order to find the reasons why you&#8217;re distraction yourself.</p>
<p>You can&#8217;t just turn off the Internet. Believe me, I&#8217;ve tried to just flip the switch and be done with it. When you&#8217;ve trained yourself to keep clicking the refresh button on your gmail, suddenly cutting the cord creates a cognitive discrepancy.</p>
<p>Your mind will expect to be checking gmail, and when it can&#8217;t plug in, it will miss the connection. If you&#8217;re relying on the connection security, you&#8217;ll feel insecure once it&#8217;s gone.</p>
<p>This is why it&#8217;s important to gradually work towards turning off the screens.</p>
<p>You could quit cold-turkey, but sometimes the shock of doing that will be too much. Especially if you&#8217;re not ready.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>Personally, I&#8217;m working towards slowly winding down my involvement on the web. Instead of reacting to every single incoming message, I&#8217;ve slowly been choosing to ignore almost everyone.</p>
<p>Instead of tweeting about everything, I&#8217;ve chosen to only tweet about what I hope will really help people.</p>
<p>Instead of taking photos of everything and flooding my Flickr/Instagr.am, I&#8217;ve started curating only the most beautiful photos.</p>
<p>Instead of responding to every single incoming question, I&#8217;ve started working directly only with people who are paying to support my work in the world.</p>
<p>I&#8217;ve been asking this question about almost everything: is this absolutely essential?</p>
<p>Increasing, the answer has been no.</p>
<p>I hope that this site can help you learn to unplug as well.</p>
<p>Maybe that&#8217;s just for five more minutes per day &#8212; maybe it&#8217;ll be for a lifetime.</p>
<p>Over the next month I&#8217;ll be publishing on the essential information that you need to start reclaiming, unplugging, and finding your center once again.</p>

<p>Maybe I&#8217;ll never come back&#8230;</p>
<p>I hope you&#8217;ll join me in exploring how we can learn to use the Internet in a mindful way in order to eventually turn off our screens.</p>
