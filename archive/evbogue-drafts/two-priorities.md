<p itemprop="description"><p>A few months ago, I read this series of articles by Jonathan Harris called <em><a href="http://www.number27.org/worldbuilding.html">World Building in a Crazy World</a></em>. Reading Jonathan’s work opened my mind to a new way of thinking about how I was interacting online.</p>
<p>It helped me begin to view my role online as being, in part, a virtual world designer. <strong>I’m not just creating words, I’m creating a world in which these words live.</strong> My website is a virtual world — not a piece of paper. </p>
<p>For some reason calling a website a virtual world lends me to take the design of it more seriously. </p>
<p>I anticipate that website design will continue to morph over time into more world-like spaces as bandwidth and technology evolves. </p>
<p>It’s very easy to let other people build a virtual world for me. But then, I have to operate under their rules. When I used to publish in Facebook, I did so cognizant of fact that the virtual world was owned by someone else. I’d read the agreement between Facebook and me: if you upload something into this world, all my data belongs to them. <strong>I gave away some freedoms</strong>, in order to operate in their virtual world.</p>
<p>When I’m creating my own virtual worlds online, there are two things that I keep in my mind:</p>
<p><strong>1. I want ownership. </strong></p>
<p>If I’m signing away the rights to my data, then I’m not building my virtual world, I’m building someone else’s.</p>
<p><strong>2. I want freedom.</strong></p>
<p>To design in a way that suits my imagination. In the current age, that means full control of CSS3 + HTML5.</p>
<p>The best way to facilitate both of these things is to build on my <a href="http://www.evbogue.com/">own domain name</a>, using a CMS (content management system) that I can install and design within. For now, that system is <a href="http://wordpress.org/">WordPress</a>. </p>
<p>//</p>
<p>I often find that it’s easier to ignore my own virtual world (here at Evbogue.com) than it is to take a hard look at every day. It’s easier for me to interact in someone else’s virtual world (like <a href="https://plus.google.com/116298207273891385223/posts">Google+</a>) than it is to invest in my own. </p>
<p>Here’s my question: <strong>how much do you invest in designing your own virtual worlds?</strong></p>
</p></div>
