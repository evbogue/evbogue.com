<p><em>February 16th 2011, San Francisco CA.</em></p>
<p><a href="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5255/5451507158_38a16b1fb0.jpg"><img class="alignleft" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5255/5451507158_38a16b1fb0.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="300"/></a>The morning that I&#8217;m writing this <a href="http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052748703373404576147922340434998.html?mod=wsj_share_twitter">Borders is filing for bankruptcy</a>.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s two days after the launch of my new book, <em><a href="http://ebookling.com/items/augmented-humanity-second-selves-mental-cybernetics-and-the-future-of-you?ref=TCUJSWPo">Augmented Humanity</a></em> on <a href="http://ebookling.com/">Ebookling</a>, a new platform for (as Colin likes to say) autherpreneurs.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s apparent to me that there has been a shift. The old model is dying and many new models (Seth Godin&#8217;s <a href="http://www.thedominoproject.com/">The Domino Project</a>, <a href="http://blog.ted.com/2011/01/26/introducing-tedbooks/">Ted Books</a>) are coming into being.</p>
<p>For a long time, in order to get something published, you had a very narrow channel of people who would get your book from idea to a store where people could buy it. Gatekeepers were the standard.</p>
<p>If you wanted to print your own book, you had to sell it on a street corner.</p>
<p>Then the Internet happened, and we were able to publish anything. And we did.</p>
<p>For the first 5,000 days of the web, every idea that dropped into our head ended up out on the web. They still do! There&#8217;s a base level of noise that most people are caught up in.</p>
<p>In order to combat the noise, many people turned to online marketers (or became marketers) in order bring their message to the world. Instead of the best books getting to their intended audience, the best marketed books tended to get to their audience. This wasn&#8217;t an ideal situation, but up until recently it was necessary to make an income as an author online.</p>
<p>Fast-forward to a new world:</p>
<p>I&#8217;ve known <a href="http://www.exilelifestyle.com/">Colin Wright</a>, the CEO of Ebookling for a over a year&#8211;we have a lot in common, such as living out of backpacks and fine taste in jackets&#8211;but we recently met in RL for the first time in Chicago over New Years. We were both quite possibly three gin-and-tonics deep when he began explaining his simple plan to distinguish <a href="http://www.ebookling.com/">Ebookling</a>.</p>
<p>&#8220;Here&#8217;s the deal.&#8221; said Colin (as I hazily recall.) &#8220;Ebookling gets 50%, authors get 50%.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;But, Colin, currently I get 50-100% and if my readers figure out E-junkie, they&#8217;ll get 50%.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;Wait, I haven&#8217;t finished yet.&#8221; said Colin. &#8220;&#8230;but, we&#8217;re going to make it super-easy for readers to affiliate for ANYTHING in the Ebookling store. All you have to do is sign up for an Ebookling account and readers can make 25% (which comes out of Ebookling&#8217;s cut). Creating an account takes 5 seconds.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;So, after you read an ebook, all you have to do is grab the affiliate link from the product page. You can Tweet it, blog it, share it on a Facebook and you have the potential to earn 25% on any product in Ebookling that you refer buys?&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;Exactly.&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;Wow.&#8221;</p>
<p>Readers could make a decent income reading ebooks?! Mind-blown wide open. All of the many connections came together to form a brighter whole new world in publishing.</p>
<p>Amazon.com gives e-book authors around 30% of the profits (unless you sell them your soul). The Apple&#8217;s iBook store is pretty similar. These companies give readers anywhere from 6%-8% in affiliate revenue &#8211;not even coffee money.</p>
<p>The other side of the spectrum is E-junkie and 1shoppingcart, which give you 100% and you choose how much your affiliates make. These companies give you a framework, you need to do all of the design and marketing. Teaching your affiliates how to sign up for E-junkie is a full-time job. One link goes to one product, so you have to cover your blog with sketchy affiliate links in order to make an income.</p>
<p>The system wasn&#8217;t ideal, but maybe Ebookling could be closer to perfection?</p>
<p>A few nights and a flight from Chicago to New York later, I met up with <a href="http://milesfitzgerald.com/">Miles Fitzgerald</a>, the COO of Ebookling for pizza and wine on Manhattan&#8217;s lower east side. I quickly brought him up to speed on the book ideas that Gwen and I were working with.</p>
<p>&#8220;Ebookling is for real? I asked.&#8221; &#8220;Yes! We&#8217;re going to launch it.&#8221; &#8220;Cool, I&#8217;ll hold you to that.&#8221; and we ordered another round of drinks.</p>
<p>A few nights later <a href="http://www.gwenbell.com/">Gwen</a>, Miles and I were gathered around the prototype of Ebookling at <a href="http://languagedept.com/home.html">The Language Dept</a> (a design firm that Gwen works closely with) on Manhattan&#8217;s west side. It looked amazing, but would it work? We needed to set a ship date, and so we did.</p>
<p>February 15th 2011: Our two e-books and Ebookling, would launch side-by-side.</p>
<p>Now we&#8217;re here. <a href="http://ebookling.com/items/augmented-humanity-second-selves-mental-cybernetics-and-the-future-of-you?ref=TCUJSWPo"><em>Augmented Humanity</em></a> and <em><a href="http://ebookling.com/items/digital-warriorship?ref=TCUJSWPo">Digital Warriorship</a></em> are live. Our dedicated blog readers, and new readers alike, are purchasing, reading, and responding the work.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s been quite a ride over the last few days. Yesterday I felt a post-launch hangover like I&#8217;d been dancing until dawn for a month and a half straight. Finally, it&#8217;s all over&#8211;and yet, we keep reminding ourselves, it has just begun.</p>
<p>Everything we do online is an experiment. Whether or not Ebookling will succeed or fail is beyond the point. To me, it&#8217;s exciting to be part of a team of people who are really making history in an age of intense uncertainty.</p>
