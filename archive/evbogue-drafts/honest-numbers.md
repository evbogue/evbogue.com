<p><i>Written by Ev Bogue | Follow me on <a href="https://plus.google.com/116298207273891385223/">Google+</a></i></p>
<p itemprop="description"><p>When I blanked the slate on <a href="/posts/farbeyondthestars">Far Beyond The Stars</a> and moved my blog over to Evbogue.com, I deleted the entire feedburner list.</p>
<p>A few thousand people re-subscribed, but nowhere near the 8,700+ people who I’d previously been transmitting to.</p>
<p>This said something specific to me: most of those people weren’t paying attention.</p>
<p>It’s not because there is anything wrong with not paying attention. It’s just that my number wasn’t right. I had an over-inflated sense of who was interested in my work. It wasn’t 8,700 people. It was less than 2,000.</p>
<p>Over the last half of year, that list has grown and changed. People have subscribed and unsubscribed. In fact, I assume that many of the people reading now never encountered my old blog.</p>
<p>Readers receive my blog posts in ways that I can’t even imagine. I imagine some people read me on <a href="http://flipboard.com/">Flipboard</a>, for example. There’s no number for that.</p>
<p><strong>I believe that real, honest, numbers are the only numbers to work with.</strong> This is why I look at my open rate on <a href="http://www.mailchimp.com/">Mailchimp</a> (not affiliate link). It’s honest with me. It might not be the most flattering number for my ego, but it’s the number of people who are actually interested.</p>
<p><strong>An open rate is a type of sensory perception for my digital self.</strong> </p>
<p>I want to actually know who reads my stuff, not an unrealistic number that’s been padding itself over time as readers come and go.</p>
<p>How do you know who’s reading your work?</p>
