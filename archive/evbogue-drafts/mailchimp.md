<p itemprop="description"><p>Recently, I received an email from someone who went to dance school years ago with me at NYU. She invited me to go to her dance show, in New York. I’m in Seattle, so I can’t attend. </p>
<p>At the bottom of her email: “If you don’t want to receive emails from me, reply to me and say: don’t send me emails!” There was no unsubscribe button.</p>
<p><a href="/posts/email">Email is my sanctuary</a>, so I feel frustrated and a little peeved when I open my email box to see that I’ve been invited to a show by someone who I haven’t seen in years, in another state. </p>
<p>I realize that you might feel that way too. This is why I wrote this blog post.</p>
<p>I don’t want to feel angry at someone who I went to school with. In fact, I wish I could go to their show. But, the reality is that I can’t keep up with everyone who I’ve ever known in the world — I’d be overwhelmed with incoming emails. So, I need to have an option to unsubscribe from their email.</p>
<p>I emailed my dancer friend back to ask if she would be willing to consider using <a href="http://eepurl.com/fs1tY">Mailchimp</a> (rewards link) for her email list. I stated the benefits. She emailed back a few moments later saying: “Yes! I’ll use it next time.”</p>
<p>//</p>
<p>In 1999, Seth Godin wrote a book called <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permission_marketing"><em>Permission Marketing</em></a>. The premise? If I gather permission from someone, they will be more willing to support me financially. </p>
<p>I gather permission, because I want to make sure that my work ends up in the hands of people who want it. I want to give them control, without talking to a human, of whether or not they want to hear from me.</p>
<p>I use Mailchimp to gather permission. It’s one of the #1 tools that I use to learn about who my audience is, and then invite them to support my work when I’m working on a new project.</p>
<p>If I don’t feel in control, then I feel angry that I’ve received an unsolicited email. I might just hit spam on it, if I receive enough of these emails.</p>
<p>//</p>
<p>What my dancer friend could have done was this: email me individually and asked the following.</p>
<p>Dear Ev,<br/>
I’m creating a list for my dance company using Mailchimp. Would you be interested in hearing updates from us? You can join the list here. If you’re still in New York, maybe you’d be interested in coming out to see us!</p>
<p>I hope you’re doing well!</p>
<p>Best,<br/>
Dancer friend</p>
<p>//</p>
<p>Here’s another experiment: put a mailchimp subscribe link at the bottom of every email you send. “Do you want to hear more from me? <a href="http://eepurl.com/eKhvg">Subscribe here</a>.”</p>
<p>//</p>
<p>I do my best to only contact people who have given me permission to contact them. If I do email someone who hasn’t given permission, I do my best to state a request clearly in order to ask for what I need. </p>
<p>If you’re interested in creating your own list, I recommend <a href="http://eepurl.com/fs1tY">Mailchimp</a> (rewards link.)</p>
<p>If you’re willing hear more from me, you can <a href="http://eepurl.com/eKhvg">subscribe here</a>. I’ll never send anything that you haven’t requested. You can unsubscribe at any time.</p>
