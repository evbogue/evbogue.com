<p>I’ve been on <a href="https://plus.google.com/116298207273891385223/">Google+</a> for a few weeks now. I’ve been <a href="/posts/twitter">off Twitter for two months</a>, and off Facebook for nine months. </p>
<p>I’m beginning to believe that Google+ is the most useful social network that’s evolved so far. I’m seeing high levels of engagement on the service. Google has done an amazing job in how the service is structured. The service has been created in a way that makes sense to a wide variety of people using in many different ways.</p>
<p>In a way, it’s a social layer that sits on top of the Internet. </p>
<p>//</p>
<p>Why Google+ is different.</p>
<p>In my experience, Google+ works differently than Twitter and Facebook. </p>
<p>In the <a href="http://www.kk.org/thetechnium/archives/2011/04/techno_life_ski.php">words of techno philosopher Kevin Kelly</a> “Often learning a new tool requires unlearning the old one. The habits of using a land line phone don’t work in email or cell phone. The habits of email don’t work in twitter. The habits of twitter won’t work in what is next.”</p>
<p>I believe Twitter and Google+ are fundamentally different. I see a lot of people using Google+ like it’s Twitter, which doesn’t work. </p>
<p>What does work on Google+? How is it different? We’re going to find that out. Unlike Facebook, it’s public. Unlike Twitter, content is longer-form and conversation based. </p>
<p>I like how there’s no need to unfollow people for the service to work properly for users who have large followings like myself (I have well over 1,000 followers at this point, and rising.) I can reciprocate by following back, but it’s easy to filter my stream at the same time in order to focus.</p>
<p>//</p>
<p>I asked on Google+ how many social networks people were on, and why they were on them.</p>
<p>Many people said they were on the three big ones. Facebook, Twitter, and Google+. Not many are on LinkedIn, because I don’t really write or hang for the professional crowd — (or even understand them much really.)</p>
<p>One of the number one reasons for not leaving Facebook was “I’m waiting for my friends to move over.”</p>
<p>I don’t believe many of my friends are going to move over from Facebook. Just like when we graduated high school and 95% of everyone stayed in high school, so will 95% of everyone stay on Facebook. They’ve bought into a service that owns their content, and won’t let it be exported. </p>
<p>In my experience, most people on Facebook don’t have a foothold on the open web (they’re not publishers.) So, they’ll probably stay there. This is okay. There’s a huge new and growing community of amazing people on Google+. This is a time to connect with new people.</p>
<p>I worry too many people are trying to divide their attention between three social Apps, and ending up with very low results. If your intention is to find new customers, then in my experience a more focused approach is probably best. </p>
<p>I’m only maintaining a presence on Google+ now.</p>
<p>The purpose of this is ultimately to bring people back to my site, it’s the home base. I’m a professional writer, so I have to constantly think about where the support from the work comes from. Am I just seeing traffic, or am I seeing cash flow? These are all open questions in my mind.</p>
<p>//</p>
<p>Anyway, these are just a few of my thoughts. There are no solid answers here, I’m simply exploring what this next evolution in social means. </p>
