<p class="date">October 9, 2011</p>
<p itemprop="description"><p>Every once in awhile, I get really obsessed with the work of someone with a blog. So much so, that I read their blog from start to finish over the course of a few days without taking much of a break.</p>
<p>Recently, I started reading <a href="http://penelopetrunk.com/">Penelope Trunk’s blog</a> obsessively from start to finish. Why? Because there’s something about her writing that <strong>eerily reminds me of myself</strong>. In some ways, <strong>I admire her brutal honesty about nearly everything</strong>. I know that I have the capacity to do the same thing, because I have. The question in my mind: is there a way to be as honest as Penelope trunk, without communicating so violently? If I could gift Penelope two things: 1. Some <a href="http://www.amazon.com/What-Say-Matters-Practicing-Communication/dp/1930485247">non-violent communications training</a>. 2. <a href="/posts/earn">A Letter</a> so she can keep some things private, have an income, and still express herself in an uncensored way.</p>
<p>Last year, I got really obsessed with <a href="http://sivers.org/blog">Derek Sivers</a>. I got so obsessed that I read his entire blog in reverse chronological order from finish to start. I learned so much doing this, I definitely suggest it. What I appreciate about Derek is that he <strong>only writes things that he understands completely</strong>.  It seems to me like he’d rather wait a long time than publish something that’s unprocessed and/or raw. He also only does things if he’s <a href="http://sivers.org/hellyeah">Hell, Yeah!</a> about them.</p>
<p>The year before that, I got really obsessed with <a href="http://sethgodin.typepad.com/">Seth Godin</a>. So much so in fact, that I read his entire blog from start to finish in chronological order. While I was obsessively reading his entire blog, I also read almost all of his books. One of the most important lessons I learned from reading Seth is <strong>how to code SEO into language</strong>. <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1591843170/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&tag=farbeythestam-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399369&creativeASIN=1591843170">Purple Cow</a>. <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00509CRG6/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&tag=farbeythestam-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399373&creativeASIN=B00509CRG6">Linchpin</a>. <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1591842336/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&tag=farbeythestam-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399369&creativeASIN=1591842336">Tribes</a>. (and even <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B002ACPM54/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&tag=farbeythestam-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399369&creativeASIN=B002ACPM54">Meatball Sundae</a>…?) These are all words that we use because of Seth. <strong>Seth’s a creator of new google-able language.</strong></p>
<p>There are other bloggers I’ve read from start to finish obsessively. I’m not mentioning them here, because they’re not as good. It takes a lot of work and life-experience to write a blog that I’ll obsessively read from start to finish.</p>
<p>The saddest part of reading a blog from start to finish is that once I’m done I have to wait for new blog posts in order to get my fill. Luckily, there’s a lot of work for me to do in-between obsessively reading brilliant bloggers.</p>
