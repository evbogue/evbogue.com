<p><a href="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5101/5692661271_4ae4dbceb8_z.jpg"><img class="alignright" src="http://farm6.static.flickr.com/5101/5692661271_4ae4dbceb8_z.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="300"/></a>Now that I&#8217;m writing <a href="http://www.letterly.net/evbogue">Letters</a> regularly, the depth of the writing requires a great deal of solitude.</p>
<p>I&#8217;ve been reading <em><a href="http://www.amazon.com/Letters-Young-Rainer-Maria-Rilke/dp/1607960265/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;qid=1304892593&amp;sr=8-1">Letters to a Young Poet</a></em> by Rainer Maria Rilke. It&#8217;s beautiful. One of the themes throughout Rilke&#8217;s work is the importance of solitude in work.</p>
<p>Solitude runs counter to the demands for attention that come as the work becomes well received.</p>
<p>The more impact the work, the more people want to email me asking for my attention.</p>
<p>These days, I&#8217;d rather be writing. Writing is a solitary endeavor. There is no collective, there is no group consensus, there is no conversation.</p>
<p>Writing is just me, my notebook, it&#8217;s just me and my keyboard, it&#8217;s just me and the publish button.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>In November, realized that I didn&#8217;t want to be expected to entertain a large audience in person.</p>
<p>It was raining in San Francisco. I took a bus to an event for <a href="http://www.chrisguillebeau.com/3x5">Chris Guillebeau</a>&#8216;s book tour. I ran into <a href="http://www.mnmlist.com/">Leo Babauta</a> on the bus. We chatted, briefly.</p>
<p>I walked into the room of 250+ people, immediately people wanted my attention.</p>
<p>&#8220;Oh, you&#8217;re Everett Bogue, the minimalist.&#8221;</p>
<p><a href="http://www.corbettbarr.com/">Corbett</a> introduced Chris. He asked how many people in the audience had sent Chris an email. All hands rose. He asked how many people got a response. All hands rose.</p>
<p>Chris looked tired of answering emails.</p>
<p>Leo spoke briefly to the crowd, and escaped the crowd back home (even more people wanted to talk to him &#8212; why wouldn&#8217;t they? Leo&#8217;s email address is secret &#8212; none of them could talk to him any other way.)</p>
<p>Me, I stayed.</p>
<p>I stayed as one person after another came up to me and picked my brain.</p>
<p>I left emotionally/physically/creatively drained.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>Maybe someday I&#8217;ll be a speaker, or someone who can work a room. For now, I write in solitude.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>I don&#8217;t give out my email address anymore. I used to, but around a year and a half ago I started to receive so many emails that responding to them took half a day.</p>
<p>I&#8217;m happy to respond to peers. I&#8217;m happy to respond to friends.</p>
<p>Everyone who signs up for <a href="http://www.letterly.net/evbogue">The Letter</a> gets access to my email address. These subscribers are committed, so I trust that they use the access mindfully.</p>
<p>But for me, the attention economy is not a reason to stay up until 3 am just to react to every email or blog comment or tweet that comes my way. Constantly responding creates an artificial sense of importance &#8212; I found this to be a distraction from the depth of my work.</p>
<p>Writing at the surface is easily forgotten, writing at depth can have lasting power.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>These days, I receive 2-6 emails a day.</p>
<p>I don&#8217;t feel pressured to respond to any of them.</p>
<p>I&#8217;m not accountable to the people who email me.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>I&#8217;m headed to Seattle to find a quiet place to write for awhile. I&#8217;m working on a new project. I&#8217;m sending work-in-progress to the Letter. Letter subscribers will get the new project free when it&#8217;s released.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>How to find a quiet place to write.</p>
<p>1. Take care of basic needs.</p>
<p>A soft futon, on the ground, in a reliable place. Two teacups. A blender for smoothies. A way to make coffee and/or tea.</p>
<p>2. Places to walk.</p>
<p>Ideas come when I take long walks. A beautiful city is walkable.</p>
<p>3. Places to write.</p>
<p>Somewhere to sit whenever the inspiration to write strikes. A coffee shop that&#8217;s quiet, with dependable Wifi, a good atmosphere, and decently roasted beans. Perhaps a corner with a soft light to write when I wake up at 3am with an idea stuck in my head.</p>
<p>4. Solitude.</p>
<p>A deep sense of aloneness. The vast distance between all of us, acknowledged.</p>
