<p itemprop="description"><p>This is the first in a series of two posts on how I’m using <a href="https://plus.google.com/116298207273891385223/">Google+</a>.</p>
<p>Don’t miss out on the second part. Sign up to receive <a href="http://eepurl.com/eKhvg">free updates in your inbox</a> (daily, or weekly!).</p>
<p>In the words of <a href="http://www.kk.org/thetechnium/archives/2011/04/techno_life_ski.php">Kevin Kelly</a>: </p>
<blockquote><p><em>“Often learning a new tool requires unlearning the old one. The habits of using a land line phone don’t work in email or cell phone. The habits of email don’t work in twitter. The habits of twitter won’t work in what is next.”</em> – Kevin Kelly</p></blockquote>
<p>I <a href="/posts/twitter">untethered from Twitter</a> 16 days before Google+ launched. At the time, I’d heard that Google was working on a social network (word had been floating around since SXSW 2011), but I didn’t know what it was going to look like. I didn’t know if I’d like it or not. I didn’t know what it looked like. Now we know.</p>
<p>I untethered from Twitter because it wasn’t working for me anymore. I wasn’t looking for a new relationship with a social network. It just so happened that one came along, and we decided to start working together.</p>
<p>With the Kevin Kelly quote in mind, I decided to approach Google+ like it was a completely new way of interacting with the Internet. Instead of applying preconceived notions gained from Livejournal, Myspace, Facebook and Twitter, (and every other service I’ve used on the Internet) I decided to do my best to blank the slate on my approach towards this new service.</p>
<p>I’ve come across a lot of people using Google+ like it’s Facebook or Twitter. While aesthetically similar, Google+ doesn’t work anything like Twitter or Facebook did. I tried using it like Twitter: posting short updates pointing towards content. No one cared. So I stopped using Google+ like Twitter. I haven’t cared much for how Facebook worked for a long time, so I haven’t used Google+ like Facebook.</p>
<p>What I’ve seen is this: the most successful people using Google+ are approaching it like it’s a new App. These people have untethered from the past in order to move into the future of digital work.</p>
<p>If a method of using a service falls flat a few times, I untether. Why keep doing something if it isn’t working?</p>
<p>What is working? I’ll cover that in the second part. <a href="http://eepurl.com/eKhvg">Are you in?</a></p>
