<p class="date">October 15, 2011</p>
<p itemprop="description"><p>When I write, I ask myself this question: what’s the most obvious thing I can write about from my experience?</p>
<p>Derek Sivers writes about this here: <a href="http://sivers.org/obvious ">Obvious to You, Amazing to Others</a>.</p>
<blockquote><p>An excerpt: “One day someone emailed me and said, “I never would have thought of that. How did you even come up with that? It’s genius!”</p>
<p>Of course I disagreed, and explained why it was nothing special.</p>
<p>But afterwards, I realized something surprisingly profound:</p>
<p>Everybody’s ideas seem obvious to them.” – Derek Sivers</p></blockquote>
<p>When I push myself to write the craziest shit imaginable, most of the time it doesn’t land. </p>
<p>When I write something obvious (to me), often it leads to amazement from people who read my work.</p>
<p>//</p>
<p>It seems obvious to that all of my <a href="/posts/personal-things">stuff fits into a bag</a>.<br/>
It seems obvious to me when <a href="/posts/write-like-you-talk">I write like I talk</a>.<br/>
It seems obvious to me that I <a href="/posts/untether-from-facebook">don’t need to use Facebook</a>.<br/>
It seems obvious to me that books are now <a href="/posts/post-artifact-booking">fluid containers for information</a>.<br/>
It seems obvious to me that <a href="/posts/how-to-create-an-experiment">everything is an experiment</a>.</p>
<p>//</p>
<p>When I write what’s obvious to me, and amazing to others, I have an easier job as a writer. </p>
<p>My writing also lands with the people who gather around my work.</p>
<p>This seems obvious to me. But is it amazing to others?</p>
