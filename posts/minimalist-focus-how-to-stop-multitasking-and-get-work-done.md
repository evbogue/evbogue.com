<p>Hey blogaudience! I&#8217;ve got a lot of work to do this week.</p>
<p>As I&#8217;m writing blog post for you to read, I&#8217;m also doing these things:</p>
<ul>
<li>Creating a brilliant logo for a creative company in California</li>
<li>Creating a stunning website for a creative company in California</li>
<li>Finishing production on a website for an artist in New York</li>
<li>Writing 15 short informative stories for a web publisher</li>
<li>Drinking a coffee</li>
<li>Checking my Twitter</li>
<li>Checking my email</li>
<li>Doing dishes</li>
<li>Doing laundry</li>
<li>Thinking about what to eat for lunch</li>
<li>Trying to sell my bike</li>
</ul>
<p>Whew, that&#8217;s a lot of work that I&#8217;ve got to do, and most of it involves a hard-to-harness skill called &#8216;Creativity&#8217;.</p>
<p>The problem is that for the last three hours I&#8217;ve been trying to do all of this stuff at the same time. I&#8217;m getting somewhere, but I&#8217;m not seeing the results that I need to see in order to get all of these projects to done in an acceptable amount of time.</p>
<p><strong>It&#8217;s a well known fact that multitasking is a productivity killer. </strong>Minimalists try not to multitask, because by doing so we&#8217;re wasting time that we could be enjoying our lives.</p>
<p><strong>So why am I engaging in the ultimate productivity sin?</strong></p>
<p>Because I haven&#8217;t stopped myself yet, which is why I&#8217;m writing this post. I&#8217;m going to turn this situation around. I&#8217;m going to be a minimalist productivity master, and so are you!</p>
<p><strong>How a minimalist can single-task his way to done.</strong></p>
<p><strong>1, Identify which of these tasks can be finished in under 2 minutes.</strong> Do these tasks first, so you don&#8217;t have to worry about them. Do them one at a time. Until all small tasks are completed. I&#8217;m going to make sure my email is back at <a href="http://www.inboxzero.com/">inbox zero</a> before I start on big projects.</p>
<p><strong>2, Figure out the first big project that you want to complete.</strong> If you need to write this project on a post-it and paste it somewhere visible, as a reminder that you shouldn&#8217;t be Tweeting.</p>
<p><strong>3, Eliminate all distractions.</strong> Turn off email, twitter, Facebook, etc. Stop reading the New York Times. Finish eating your sandwich. Lock yourself in a room and don&#8217;t come out until you need to eat.</p>
<p><strong>4, Stop doing other projects while you&#8217;re working on this project. </strong>I find myself googling research material for my short informative stories, but I should wait until I&#8217;m actually writing them. We&#8217;ll get to <em>other things</em> when the time comes, right now you need to focus on <em>this thing.</em></p>
<p><strong>5, Break down the larger project into actionable tasks. </strong>It&#8217;s important to break big projects down into smaller task that you can do in a reasonable amount of time. If you don&#8217;t you risk being paralyzed by the scope of what you need to accomplish. For instance, I&#8217;m going to designing that logo with an outline, then create a 3D object, then play with color, then play with texture.</p>
<p><strong>4, </strong><strong>Work on the project that you choose until it&#8217;s complete, or until you need to take a break.</strong> Save it, publish it, send it on to your boss. Good, it&#8217;s done.</p>
<p><strong>5, </strong><strong>Repeat from step 2 until everything on your to-do list is done.</strong></p>
<p>Yay! Now we&#8217;re minimalist productivity masters! Now let me get to that logo design.</p>
