<p>From an early age you&#8217;ve been indoctrinated into a society that values things above people.</p>
<p>We are what we own, or so you&#8217;ve been told.</p>
<p>They all told you that you wanted the two-car garage. They told you to fill one side with stuff you couldn&#8217;t fit inside your house. Indeed, <a href="http://www.becomingminimalist.com/2010/01/19/the-statistics-of-clutter/">one quarter</a> of all Americans have a two-car garage in this condition.</p>
<p>The stuff just sits there. You walk by it, and wish that it would disappear. Secretly you wish someone would burn your two-car garage down, so you won&#8217;t have to make the decision to get rid of that clutter.</p>
<p>We find so many ways to keep us from reaching our potential. Stuff is just one of those ways. We don&#8217;t want to deal with the harsh reality of our lives, the fact that we haven&#8217;t really done anything important.</p>
<p>So we refocuse all of our attention on the endless burden of resorting our stamp collection.</p>
<p>A friend of mine, the brooklyn hip-hop artist D.O.V. of <a href="http://verbalgraffiticulture.com/">Verbal Graffiti</a>, repeatedly loses his life work every couple of years. In 2003 his house burned down. In 2009 his laptop was stolen from his living room without a trace, containing years of un-backed-up recordings.</p>
<p>The loss is always devastating to him. He tells himself that this is the end of his career and he&#8217;ll never make another beat again.</p>
<p>But this wasn&#8217;t the case, there was no devastating repercussions. Six months after the loss of his computer he had a new album on the streets.</p>
<p>The beats weren&#8217;t on his computer, they were in him. By clearing away all the years of junk on his computer&#8211;all of the beats that never had any potential, but he continued to mess with,&#8211;he was able to free himself to create a new album.</p>
<p>A clean slate can be a powerful drive to create.</p>
<p>What if you were able to harness this ability for a lifetime?</p>
