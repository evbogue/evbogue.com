<p><em>Every once in awhile I interview an important person on the subject of being minimalist. A couple of prominent minimalists I&#8217;ve interviewed in the post: <a href="http://www.farbeyondthestars.com/?p=569">Leo Babauta</a> on the liberation of being minimalist, and <a href="http://www.farbeyondthestars.com/?p=504">Colin Wright</a> about working from anywhere in sexy shoes.</em></p>
<p><a href="/assets/posts/an-interview-with-karol-gajda-incredible-lightness-of-traveling-img1.jpg"><img class="alignleft size-full wp-image-915" title="karol" src="/assets/posts/an-interview-with-karol-gajda-incredible-lightness-of-traveling-img1.jpg" alt="" width="250" height="250"/></a>This week I&#8217;ve had the pleasure of talking to Karol Gajda, the blogger behind <a href="http://www.ridiculouslyextraordinary.com/">Ridiculously Extraordinary</a>. Karol is a perpetual traveler, living out of a bag and working from everywhere. He leaves for India in a few days. He hopes to help 100 people begin to live extraordinary lives through his blog.</p>
<p>We discussed tips for traveling lighter and supporting yourself while you&#8217;re on the road.</p>
<p><strong>Everett Bogue:</strong> <em>What made you decide to live a ridiculously extraordinary life?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol Gajda:</strong> It was either that or continue being normal.</p>
<p>But seriously, initially my only goal was to not have to get a job out of college by working for myself full time. That was almost 10 years ago. I didn&#8217;t really take advantage of the Ridiculously Extraordinary freedom I had by traveling extensively until recently.</p>
<p><strong>Everett: </strong><em>You&#8217;re headed to India in a few weeks, according to your travel itinerary on your blog. Can you give us a short rundown of your travel plans?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol:</strong> I have a one way ticket and very general plans. I don&#8217;t even have my accommodations set yet.</p>
<p>My first month will be in Goa. A guy from the UK set up a little guitar building school there and I&#8217;m going to build my own guitar. If I love Goa I&#8217;ll stay there for a while after that.</p>
<p>Otherwise I&#8217;m not sure. I may visit Thailand after India, but again, it&#8217;s all up in the air. I am definitely visiting Poland when it starts warming up. I was born in Poland, but haven&#8217;t spent an extensive amount of time there. I do speak the language, but I&#8217;m looking forward to learning to speak much better.</p>
<p><strong>Everett:</strong> <em>I recently watched your <a href="http://manvsdebt.com/do-shit-you-love-karol-gadja/">interview with Baker</a>, where you discussed traveling with three quick-dry shirts.  Can you explain more about your approach to clothing while you&#8217;re traveling?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol:</strong> My approach is simple: take the absolute bare minimum and <a href="http://www.ridiculouslyextraordinary.com/15-minutes-clean-clothes-anywhere-in-the-world/">hand wash everything</a>.</p>
<p>Having less makes my life easy. My single pair of convertible pants may look funny, but I don&#8217;t have to think about what I&#8217;m going to wear tomorrow.</p>
<p><strong>Everett:</strong> <em>What other essentials do you travel with?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol:</strong> My laptop so I can get work done. I do enjoy seeing the sights, of course, but I also love working in new places. To some that defeats the purpose of travel. But that&#8217;s the beauty of being free. I can do what I want.</p>
<p>If I want to work on the road perpetually I can. If I want to come back &#8220;home&#8221; (which is nowhere at the moment) I can. I&#8217;m not bound by the limits of savings. That said, I&#8217;m a pretty budget conscious traveler.</p>
<p><strong>Everett:</strong> <em>Can you recommend one way that we can all travel lighter?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol:</strong> Yes, my best tip if you want to travel light is to get a smaller carry on. Preferably a backpack. Mine is 32L. Then force yourself to fit everything you need in there.</p>
<p><strong>Everett:</strong> <em>One of the hardest aspects of traveling is being able to support yourself financially on the road, how do you accomplish this?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol:</strong> I&#8217;ve been working online for myself for almost 10 years now. It&#8217;s a little bit more difficult while traveling because of sometimes spotty Internet and because there are a lot of cool things to see and people to hang out with. I&#8217;ve found that it&#8217;s incredibly difficult if I&#8217;m constantly on the move so I&#8217;ve reassessed my travel goals. From now on I&#8217;m staying in each new place for at least a month. (Unless, of course, I just don&#8217;t like it.)</p>
<p><strong>Everett:</strong> <em>Can you suggest one method we can employ to support themselves financially while we&#8217;re traveling abroad?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol</strong>: That&#8217;s a great question, and one that&#8217;s at the forefront of a lot of prospective traveler&#8217;s minds. A lot of travelers I met in Australia saved up enough to travel for a few months and then found jobs along the way to extend the travel.</p>
<p>If you don&#8217;t already have an online business and want to get started traveling right away, I think that&#8217;s the easiest way.</p>
<p><strong>Everett:</strong> <em>When you first started traveling, did you bring anything with you that you thought was a necessity, but it turned out not to be?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol:</strong> Well, a couple of years ago I went on a 3 week US tour with my friend&#8217;s band, and <a href="http://www.ridiculouslyextraordinary.com/what-its-like-to-tour-with-a-rock-band/">I took a full suitcase</a>. Not a small one either. I think I packed a full 2 weeks of clothes. 14 shirts, 14 underwear, 14 socks! Plus an extra pair of pants and 2 hoodies.</p>
<p>I&#8217;ve always been fairly minimalistic in other aspects of my life so I don&#8217;t think I ever had any other extras to take besides too many clothes.</p>
<p><strong>Everett: </strong><em>And finally, can you think of one aspect of our lives that many of us can change, which can help us lead to life that is more free?</em></p>
<p><strong>Karol</strong>: It&#8217;s difficult to state something that everybody needs to change. And my goals aren&#8217;t really aligned with telling people what to do. The most important advice I can offer someone is to take action. If you want to start a business, start it. If you want to write a book, write it.</p>
<p>If you want to travel the world, buy a ticket and go.</p>
