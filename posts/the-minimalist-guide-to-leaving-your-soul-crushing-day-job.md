<p><em>This is  the first of a three part series on using minimalism to leave your day  job in order to live and work anywhere.<br/>
</em></p>

<p><a href="/assets/posts/the-minimalist-guide-to-leaving-your-soul-crushing-day-job-img1.jpg"><img class="alignleft" src="/assets/posts/the-minimalist-guide-to-leaving-your-soul-crushing-day-job-img1.jpg" alt="" width="263" height="350"/></a>If  you&#8217;ve been following this blog long, or read <a href="http://artofbeingminimalist.com/"><em>The Art of Being  Minimalist</em></a>, you know that I left my job last August in order to launch my  minimalist business and live and work from anywhere.</p>
<p>If you&#8217;re in a situation like I was a year ago, &#8211;the  monotonous repetitive days, the future of my creativity rapidly dying,&#8211; I  imagine you want to do this too.</p>
<p>You  want to be like <a href="http://www.exilelifestyle.com/">Colin Wright</a>, and country hop every four months. Or like  <a href="http://www.ridiculouslyextraordinary.com/">Karol Gajda</a>, making a reasonable living online while crafting a  hand-made guitar in India. Maybe you want to be like <a href="http://www.rowdykittens.com/">Tammy Strobel</a> and  start a very small writing business to support your <a href="https://www.e-junkie.com/ecom/gb.php?cl=100096&amp;c=ib&amp;aff=91858">car-free lifestyle</a>.</p>
<p>Maybe you want to be like you! That&#8217;s even better.</p>
<p><strong>It doesn&#8217;t matter what ideal life you  imagine, you just need to know that it&#8217;s possible.</strong></p>
<p><em>Before I get started:</em> whenever I write these types of  things, I always get comments from two kinds of people who think I&#8217;m  nuts.</p>
<p>The first is the people with  kids, &#8220;oh it&#8217;s so hard, I could never do that&#8221; crowd.</p>
<p>I know, it&#8217;s so much easier to quit your job when you&#8217;re  single and in your twenties, but it&#8217;s not impossible to change your life just because you decided to procreate. <a href="http://zenhabits.net/">Leo Babauta</a> started his own business and quit his job  through minimalism, and he has six kids! You can too, no excuses!</p>
<p>The other group of people who comment are the ones who  claim to love their job.</p>
<p>Great! I&#8217;m so happy for you, don&#8217;t change  anything.</p>
<p><em>But,</em> if you really love your job, why are you reading a blog post  about leaving your job? Go read and comment on something else! &#8230;unless  you actually secretly hate your job, in which case you need to ask  yourself some hard questions. Don&#8217;t just deny everything until you  wake up one day 15 years down the road and wonder where your life went.</p>
<p>Now then, let&#8217;s get to business&#8230;</p>
<p><strong>The obstacles of leaving your job.</strong></p>
<p>Quitting your job is never easy. There are  a number of obstacles to overcome in order to even think of going out  on your own.</p>
<p><strong>1</strong><strong>. Overcome your fear of  certain death.</strong></p>
<p>Everyone told me that if  I quit my job during the greatest recession, I&#8217;d end up living in a mud  hut down on the other side of town swigging malt liquor out if a sipper  cup.</p>
<p>This is the opposite of true.  I&#8217;ve found that the biggest growth opportunities are here, right now.  Everything about the way we&#8217;re doing business is diversifying immensely.  The time to start your own very small business is now, as there have  never been more opportunities to reach out and find the tribe that will  support your goals.</p>
<p>So ignore every  horror story that you hear. These people are trying desperately to  keep you from making a change &#8211;and who can blame them? If you can do it, it looks badly on them if they&#8217;ve settled for mediocrity.</p>
<p>Don&#8217;t listen to their pleas to be  realistic.</p>
<p>The worst possible thing that could happen to you, if you do this, is probably not nearly as bad as you think. It&#8217;s really hard to fail hard in our society, as long as you have some basic common sense about you.</p>
<p><strong>2. Realize that you&#8217;re  going to need new non-work friends.</strong></p>
<p>I&#8217;ve lost  touch with every single friend I had at my old job &#8212; except the ones  who left too. The common bonds that create an instant social network at a  job are shallow indeed. When you&#8217;re talking about entrepreneurship, and  they&#8217;re talking about maintaining the status-quo, this creates an instant barrier to communications.</p>
<p>Automatically  assume that anyone who you work with now is not going to go out of  their way to support your quest for freedom. Find help elsewhere, meet  other people who have made this journey &#8212; the Internet is a great place  to do this&#8211; these people are invaluable, and will tell you not to  settle when you&#8217;re thinking or giving up.</p>
<p>That being said, some people will support you! That&#8217;s great, don&#8217;t fire your friends if they&#8217;re helpful. Fire them if they&#8217;re holding you back by telling you that you can&#8217;t succeed.</p>
<p><strong>3. Dare to dream unrealistically.<br/>
</strong></p>
<p>I wrote recently about the need to be  <a href="http://www.farbeyondthestars.com/?p=1224">completely unrealistic</a>. You need to write down an unrealistic goal and start to  live and breathe it every single day. This can be simple, or more complex. Make it crazy though! The sky is the limit, and trust me, people have been up there too.</p>
<p>Everything crazy has been done already, so you might as well do it again.</p>
<p>My  goal was to become a minimalist in order to live and work from  anywhere. It wasn&#8217;t nearly as hard as I thought it would be. My primary income source is this blog, which I never thought would happen this quickly &#8212; the income potential to earn money online is enormous. You can reach almost anyone.</p>
<p>Your dream doesn&#8217;t have to be about making money online, but you do need to have some sort of goal.</p>
<p><strong>4. Be confident when presenting your ideas to friends and strangers.</strong></p>
<p>One of the biggest challenges, when deciding to leave a day job, is the opinions of others.</p>
<p>When you tell your best friend that you&#8217;re opting out of the rat-race to pursue a career as a writer, they will look at you like you&#8217;re a nutcase. It&#8217;s okay that they have doubts, you&#8217;re making a change and it&#8217;s only natural for them to worry.</p>
<p>That&#8217;s why it&#8217;s important to present your plans with confidence. Don&#8217;t hesitate or shake uncontrollably in fear when you tell people of your plans. Just say in a firm voice, with confidence, that this is the path you intend to tread.</p>
<p>I shared my unrealistic dream of becoming a location independent writer with people, initially they thought I was crazy! Six months later, I&#8217;m making a full time living. I&#8217;m no longer crazy.</p>
<p><strong>5. Don&#8217;t let others decide your fate.</strong></p>
<p>Ultimately, your decision to make a change is up to you. No amount of deliberation with friends and family will make your decision easier. In fact, the more you talk the harder it will be to do something.</p>
<p>Don&#8217;t spend a year trying to decide to make a change, just do it.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p>Now that you&#8217;ve overcome some preliminary obstacles, it&#8217;s time to prepare for your departure.</p>
<p><strong>Things to do before you jump.</strong></p>
<p><strong>1. Figure out your cash flow.</strong></p>
<p>You need to start figuring out ways to  make a small amount of money outside your main job.</p>
<p>Unless you&#8217;re crazy,  like me, it&#8217;s best to have at least your basic expenses covered before  you make a jump.</p>
<p>Start by trying to  make $10 online doing something other than selling your stuff on Craigslist. It sounds like a small goal, but that&#8217;s the biggest hump. If  you can sell one digital or even physical product or service, chances  are you can scale that.</p>
<p>Once you&#8217;ve  made your first $10, try to make $10 a day. Then scale up from  there until it&#8217;s $100 a day, then $10,000 and so on.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s important to have the  objective in sight when you&#8217;re thinking about new cash flow. If you want  to make passive income on a digital product, like I do, make that  your goal and go straight to working on the product.</p>
<p>Don&#8217;t mess around  in other areas outside your focus, unless you realize that your product isn&#8217;t going to sell more than 5 copies.</p>
<p>There are of course many other ways to make money outside of the online world, that&#8217;s just where I make money, so I used it as an example. I also believe it&#8217;s a lot easier to make money online than it is in the real world in the current economic climate.</p>
<p><strong>2. Save up enough  to survive until you actually have cash flow.</strong></p>
<p>If you don&#8217;t  have time to get cash flow going, or just have no idea what you&#8217;re doing  (I was in this boat when I left), at least save up enough to cover you  expenses for a few months while you figure out what you&#8217;re doing.</p>
<p>Most small business gurus recommend a 6-month cushion. 12-months if you&#8217;re a rock star.</p>
<p>Start by getting your finances in order. If keeping track of your spending scares the crap out of you, I recommend reading Adam Baker&#8217;s brilliant e-book <a href="https://www.e-junkie.com/ecom/gb.php?cl=80780&amp;c=ib&amp;aff=91858"><em>Unautomate Your Finances</em></a> in order to get a  handle on how much you&#8217;re actually spending every month.</p>
<p>If you spend a lot of money every month, you&#8217;re going to need to cut back.</p>
<p>My ideal living expenses are around $1400 a month now &#8211;this isn&#8217;t to say I&#8217;m not making and spending a lot more than that, this is just what it costs for me to survive in Brooklyn.</p>
<p>When I was  in Portland I spent around $900 a month on living and eating.</p>
<p>When I left my job, I&#8217;d saved up $3000 and lived on that for three months. You might need more  or less depending on your living expenses.</p>
<p><strong>The less you have to spend, the larger your chance of success. </strong></p>
<p>Worrying about how little money you have to pay for stupid stuff will weigh on your mind and destroy your chances of striking out on your own.</p>
<p>When you work at a day job, you get used to having that steady stream of cash coming in every month. The more you make, the more you rely on. You need to break that cycle now, and start stashing away every last penny, or you&#8217;ll never be able to leave.</p>
<p>How can you cut down your expenses so that they&#8217;re reasonable?</p>
<p><strong>3. Apply minimalism to your life.</strong></p>
<p>Cut back on everything before you quit. Initially you&#8217;re  going to be making a lot less than you did when you were employed. Go  <a href="https://www.e-junkie.com/ecom/gb.php?cl=100096&amp;c=ib&amp;aff=91858">car-free</a>. Rent your house to strangers. Sell all of your furniture.  Cancel every single subscription &#8212; especially cable TV, then sell your  TV. Call your phone company and reduce yourself to a basic plan.</p>
<p>Do this until your only expenses are  eating and renting a small apartment.</p>
<p>Eventually  you&#8217;ll be making enough from your new business to spend more, but it&#8217;s  entirely unnecessary to scale back up after you downsize like this.</p>
<p>The  stuff keeps you down, rooted to one place, and completely ineffective.</p>
<p><strong>You can&#8217;t pursue your dreams if you&#8217;re  surrounded by crap.</strong></p>
<p>I&#8217;m not saying you should go all monk on  us, but realistically consider living with your 100 best possessions,  and nothing more. This will make you more flexible, so you can move  whenever you want and focus entirely on your business when you need  to.</p>
