<p><a href="/assets/posts/how-to-find-your-minimalist-edge-img1.jpg"><img class="alignleft" src="/assets/posts/how-to-find-your-minimalist-edge-img1.jpg" alt="" width="263" height="350"/></a>I practice yoga regularly. On most days you&#8217;ll find me either on my mat at home, or at Yoga to the People &#8212; a donation-based yoga studio in Manhattan.</p>
<p><strong>There is a place in Yoga that I call The Edge. </strong></p>
<p>The Edge is a very special place, because it&#8217;s the balance between trying too hard in a pose, and not trying enough (checking out.) You&#8217;re challenging yourself to go deep enough into the position, but not striving so hard that you&#8217;ve gone too far.</p>
<p>You can hurt yourself if you go past The Edge before you&#8217;re ready.</p>
<p>If you hurt yourself, there&#8217;s a large possibility that you will undo all of the work that you&#8217;ve done so far.</p>
<p>The Edge is different for everyone. The Edge changes day to day. You&#8217;re constantly striving to find your Edge in any Yoga position.</p>
<p><strong>I imagine The Edge exists in minimalism as well. </strong></p>
<p>Before I practiced Yoga, I spent a number of years striving to be a professional dancer.</p>
<p>Dance is very different from Yoga. While they are both physical activities, the concept of watching The Edge isn&#8217;t quite as prevalent. In Yoga the aim is to do the work, it doesn&#8217;t matter if you can grab your foot and bend it back over your head. Some people can do this, some cannot, no one will hit you with a stick if you can&#8217;t touch your toes in Yoga.</p>
<p><strong>Dance is the opposite. If you can&#8217;t bend your leg back over your head, you&#8217;re a failure. </strong></p>
<p>The reason for this is quite simple: only the top 1% of dancers get paid. Dance is a performance-based medium, so your singular aim in life is to make it to the stage. If you don&#8217;t make it to the stage, you&#8217;re just an amateur.</p>
<p>In many cases, this means that dancers don&#8217;t watch The Edge as they move toward their end goal of being on the stage.</p>
<p>This leads to injuries, eating disorders, and a lot of pain and frustration.</p>
<p>A dancer wakes up every morning, looks into the mirror, and says themselves &#8220;damn, I&#8217;m getting older and fatter, and I&#8217;ll never be Baryshnikov.&#8221;</p>
<p>You have to watch your Edge, even as a dancer (and even if very few dance teachers are aware that The Edge exists.)</p>
<p>It&#8217;s easy to look at the fact that I&#8217;m living with 50-Things and assume that I just dropped everything all at once. This isn&#8217;t true at all.</p>
<p><strong>I slowly worked towards The Edge of minimalist existence.</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>In 2003 I moved in a Truck.</li>
<li>In 2007 I moved in a Honda Civic.</li>
<li>In 2009 I moved with three bags.</li>
<li>In 2010 I will move with one bag.</li>
</ul>
<p>I slowly reduced my possessions. I created boxes of stuff that I thought I didn&#8217;t need and put them in the corner for weeks until I was sure it was time for them to go.</p>
<p>I&#8217;ve structured my life so that my work is done in either the virtual realm, or I&#8217;m working with only my body.</p>
<p>My point is this: I&#8217;ve been pushing my minimalist Edge for my entire life. I&#8217;ve been working, and reworking the practice until I&#8217;m comfortable with living with less.</p>
<p>I&#8217;m sure some people have gone from McMansion to backpack in one day, but I certainly didn&#8217;t do that. I fear if a person changed their life that drastically, they&#8217;d be going past The Edge.</p>
<p><strong>If you go past The Edge in minimalism, there&#8217;s a good chance you&#8217;ll hurt yourself. </strong></p>
<p>That being said, you need to be conscious of the end goal.</p>
<p>Minimalism isn&#8217;t minimalism if you aren&#8217;t actually practicing. There&#8217;s such a thing as not reaching your Edge. If you don&#8217;t reach your Edge, you aren&#8217;t reaching your full potential.</p>
<p>Minimalism is such an abstract concept. It can really apply to any number of things. You can get lost activities such as clearing off you desk, or re-organizing your bookshelf, and then smiling to yourself and calling yourself a rockstar minimalist. You are a minimalist! But you&#8217;re missing the point.</p>
<p><strong>There&#8217;s a point when you aren&#8217;t pushing the edge.</strong></p>
<p>Sometimes you aren&#8217;t making the effort. You&#8217;re just settled down, and waiting for something to happen. This, in my opinion, is most of society.</p>
<p>These people are constantly consuming endless amounts of junk, putting it in their houses, wondering why they aren&#8217;t happy. It&#8217;s sad, really.</p>
<p>I know, I&#8217;ve been there. I spent an entire year completely checked out at my day job. I drank too much and gained twenty pounds of belly fat. I had days during that time where my minimalist ambitions included gathering up six-packs of empty beer bottles and multiple take-out packages from the floor of my room in Brooklyn.</p>
<p>We&#8217;ve all been down that road, but there&#8217;s a point where you have to take a look what you&#8217;re aiming for. To set a goal and push your Edge consciously until you actually reach it.</p>
<p><strong>At some point you have to set goals for yourself.</strong></p>
<p>One day I simply set a goal: I was going to move across the country and start working for myself, or I&#8217;d die trying.</p>
<p>To do that I knew that I needed to reduce my possessions to less than 100 things. I didn&#8217;t do this because I thought I&#8217;d one-up all of the other minimalists &#8211;I obviously didn&#8217;t anyway. I didn&#8217;t do this to make a statement about society &#8211;though apparently people have told me that I have.</p>
<p>I made the decision to have less than 100 things and fit everything in the bag, because it was the only possible way that I could see for me to succeed. I wouldn&#8217;t have been able to start my own business, and be an average American consumer at the same time. Maybe you can! Good.</p>
<p>Last year I had a difficult choice to make: life-time servitude to the system that wants me to be in debt and buy buy buy until I die, or minimalist freedom. I opted for freedom.</p>
<p><strong>I couldn&#8217;t have done this without taking minimalism to the top 1%. </strong></p>
<p>I&#8217;m not saying that to gloat, I&#8217;m not saying that because I have less stuff than you.<a href="http://zenhabits.net/2010/04/minimalism-rethunk/"> Who cares how much stuff I have</a>, I&#8217;m not trying to one-up any of you.</p>
<p>The thing is, I wouldn&#8217;t have been able to accomplish what I did, without going all the way to The Edge. I need to go to this place of less at this moment in time.</p>
<p>Yes, this means that:</p>
<p><del>I probably won&#8217;t live with 50-things forever.</del></p>
<p><del>I probably won&#8217;t live and work from anywhere forever.</del></p>
<p>Sooner or later I might find a new challenge to pursue. (But it won&#8217;t be learning how to buy stupid stuff.)</p>
<p>[UPDATED: Actually, screw the people who <a href="http://drupagliassotti.com/2010/02/21/why-i%E2%80%99m-wary-of-100-thing-minimalism/">criticize counting things</a>.</p>
<p>We need to stand up for what we believe in: living with less is better.</p>
<p>There is nothing obsessive about having less (and making a point of showing people.) What is unhealthy: having tons of stuff. Stuff holds you down, it keeps you from being free and pursuing your dreams. </p>
<p>Living with 50 things and being location independent is pretty damn awesome. I wouldn't be able to do this and have buckets of junk.</p>
<p>In response to <a href="http://www.yousimplified.net/2010/04/25/100-things-challenge-open-letter-rant/">Charley Forness's thoughtful rant</a>.]</p>
<p><strong>I do enjoy living with less at the moment.</strong></p>
<p>Part of me knows, that you can&#8217;t really know a thing until you&#8217;ve gone all the way with it.</p>
<ul>
<li>You haven&#8217;t known dance until you&#8217;ve pushed yourself to The Edge on a stage in front of a full house.</li>
<li>You haven&#8217;t known Yoga until you&#8217;ve pushed yourself to The Edge through an entire sequence, collapsed into Savasana and passed out from blissfulness.</li>
<li>You haven&#8217;t known minimalism until all of your possessions fit into a backpack (my Edge) and hopped on a plane to a place you&#8217;ve never been before.</li>
</ul>
<p>Maybe your Edge right now is cleaning off your desk and donating a few books. Donate those books, but make sure you&#8217;re pushing your Edge when you do it. If the junk just comes back you&#8217;re not making progress.</p>
<p>Maybe your Edge is <a href="http://www.farbeyondthestars.com/?p=1216">simplifying your time</a> in order to work for yourself by <a href="http://www.farbeyondthestars.com/?p=1338">generating passive income</a>.</p>
<p>The point is, The Edge is different for everyone.</p>
<p>Maybe your Edge will never get to the point where you&#8217;re hopping on planes. That&#8217;s okay! But you have to push yourself to The Edge (but not too far.) or you&#8217;ll never see progress.</p>
