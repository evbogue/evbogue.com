<p>One of the things that I&#8217;ve been focusing on exclusively, since quitting my job in July and moving across the United States to Portland, has been focusing on what&#8217;s important.</p>
<p><strong>How does one decide what&#8217;s important?</strong></p>
<p>1, Does this positively effect my life in the future?<br/>
2, Will this bring meaning to my actions?<br/>
3, Will this accomplish something, like furthering my goals?<br/>
4, Can I look back on what I did and be proud of myself?</p>
<p><strong>And what do I think about when I am doing something that doesn&#8217;t fit my definition of important?</strong></p>
<p>1, Am I wasting my time?<br/>
2, Is this really bringing value to my life?<br/>
3, Does my body think this feels good?<br/>
4, Does my mind think this feels good?</p>
<p>Now, these aren&#8217;t catch all answers. You&#8217;ll develop your own, based on your own priorities, which vary greatly from one person to the next.</p>
<p><strong>My priorities are very simple:</strong></p>
<p>1, To do yoga daily<br/>
2, To live a life of value<br/>
3, To take care of myself, and by doing so take care of others<br/>
4, To consume as little as possible</p>
<p>So far, I think I&#8217;m doing well. I do yoga daily. Every morning I feel like I&#8217;m valuing my life, and every night I go to bed feeling like I value my life. I&#8217;m taking care of myself by not eating bad things, and in doing so I&#8217;m saving a lot of money. I made a healthy sandwich yesterday for 50 cents! Portland is so inexpensive.</p>
<p>I consume as little as possible. Now this is really key, I feel like a big percentage of my re-directional focus is maintaining the sparse and simple life that I&#8217;ve been living so far. A lot of the problems that society faces in modern times is due to America&#8217;s over-consumption of just about everything.</p>
<p>I feel that by abstaining from this practice of consumerism, I&#8217;m both improving the world and becoming an example by which other people can choose to live their lives.</p>
<p>By consuming less, I also have to make a lot less money, and thus have to work a lot less, and then I have free time to do what is important. See the wisdom here?</p>
<p><strong>How you can start escaping consumerism and focus on what is important:</strong></p>
<p><strong>1, Adopt a 30-day watch list for any item that you&#8217;re tempted to buy.</strong><br/>
A lot of consumer is snap decisions that are based on marketing hype and societal conditioning. I&#8217;ve known a lot of people who are subject to this, they&#8217;ve every moment of their lives focused on stuff, and getting more stuff, their brains are conditioned to buy buy buy.</p>
<p>You can stop this cycle by, as Leo Babauta suggests in his <a href="http://www.getrichslowly.org/blog/2009/10/09/minimalist-money-6-steps-to-simplify-your-financial-life/">guest post yesterday on Get Rich Slowly</a>, adopting a 30-day list.</p>
<p>When you&#8217;re in the store, and you say to yourself: &#8220;gee, I could use that new deluxe sandwich slicer&#8221; stop yourself, pull out your notebook and turn to the page where your 30-day list is. Write the date, write the time, write what you want to buy.</p>
<p>After 30 days, return to the list. Do you still want the deluxe sandwich slicer? No? Good, now you haven&#8217;t bought it. Do this for everything, except necessities like food, which 30 days from now you won&#8217;t really need anymore if you put it on the list.</p>
<p><strong>2, If you had to go today, could you take it all with you?</strong><br/>
I always like to think leaving. Maybe there&#8217;s a big disaster, and your city is sinking into the ground. Or you find out that you got a job in Cincinnati that is going to make your dreams come true, but they won&#8217;t pay relocating fees and you have to be there tomorrow. Or your girlfriend wants to move to Japan, tomorrow. Will it be a big project to move, or will you just pack a bag and go?</p>
<p>I can pack a bag and go, because all of my stuff fits in a bag (well, three bags: one backpacker bag, one computer bag, and one camera bag.) Isn&#8217;t that nice? It really is. I can go anywhere, whenever I want.</p>
<p><strong>3, Limit your exposure to advertising.</strong><br/>
A lot of buying is the result of ads leading to you to believe that you want something that you don&#8217;t really need. By blocking ads, you&#8217;ll negate their effects instantly. Cut down on your TV, install <a href="http://adblockplus.org/en/">Adblock for Firefox</a>, don&#8217;t look at billboards (and if you do, laugh what they&#8217;re trying to accomplish.)</p>
<p>This is really difficult, and it won&#8217;t happen overnight. You&#8217;re being subjected to literally billions of dollars of research by advertising firms on how to manipulate people, every time you look at an advertisement. It&#8217;s a big battle to fight, but one that is extremely rewarding.</p>
<p><strong>4, Breathe.</strong><br/>
If absolutely must buy something, at least stop yourself and take ten deep breathes. Walk around the store and meditate on the item you &#8220;really must have this instant&#8221;. Do you really need it now? How will it benefit your life? Will it actually hurt your life? Maybe it will, maybe it won&#8217;t. It probably will, so think about that has you walk and breathe. You might be surprised what answers come to you.</p>
