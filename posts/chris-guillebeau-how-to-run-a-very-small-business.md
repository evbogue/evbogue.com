<p><a href="http://www.flickr.com/photos/chrisguillebeau/4209808479/"><img class="alignleft size-full wp-image-1106" title="chris" src="/assets/posts/chris-guillebeau-how-to-run-a-very-small-business-img1.jpg" alt="" width="250" height="250"/></a>Chris Guillebeau is one of the more remarkable people on earth. He&#8217;s risen to <a href="http://chrisguillebeau.com/3x5/overnight-success/">&#8216;overnight&#8217; blogging fame</a>, inked a book deal, visited over 125 countries, and he wants to teach you how to achieve world domination.</p>
<p>It&#8217;s not hard to see why so many people are part of his unconventional community; his ideas actually work. I was a skeptic, and then I tried it myself. You can start a <a href="http://www.unconventionalguides.com/">Very Small Business</a> and start <a href="http://www.farbeyondthestars.com/?p=1004">producing passive income</a> in a very short time.</p>
<p>Today I&#8217;m excited to present you with the interview I did with Chris as he was flying out of Manila airport to Papua  New Guinea last week.</p>
<p>We spoke about strategies for success, and some of the common mistakes people make when launching a Very Small Business.</p>
<p>&#8211;</p>
<p><em><strong>Everett Bogue:</strong> Chris, you and I are both running Very Small Businesses, but can you take a moment to define what a Very Small business is for our readers?</em></p>
<p><strong>Chris Guillebeau:</strong> I  think of it as a for-profit project that exists to a) create value for  happy customers and b) provide a steady income for the business owner  without becoming like a job for him or her. Very Small Businesses tend  to have no employees other than the other, or perhaps one or two  additional employees. In other words, it&#8217;s a lifestyle business instead  of a business focused primarily on growth.</p>
<p>I also think of the  following characteristics as being part of a successful Very Small  Business:</p>
<ul>
<li>Location Independent (can be operated from  anywhere)</li>
<li>Some Degree of Passive Income (don&#8217;t trade time for  money)</li>
<li>Healthy profit margins (don&#8217;t compete on price!)</li>
</ul>
<p>These  characteristics are optional and don&#8217;t apply to everyone, but it will  be easier to get a new venture going if they are met. Another optional  characteristic (but important for many of us) is connecting the business  to a cause greater than itself. In my case I am working with <em>Charity:  Water</em> on a project to raise funds for <a id="zidj" title="water wells in  Ethiopia" href="http://charitywater.org/aonc">water wells in Ethiopia</a>.</p>
<p><strong> Everett: </strong><em>How can starting a  Very Small Business help you leave your day job?</em></p>
<p><strong>Chris:</strong> The most  important thing in starting a business is reducing your dependency on  the day job, whether or not you leave it. But naturally, if you can  replace at least 50% of your income through a side project, you might  want to think seriously about taking the leap.<br/>

<p><strong> Everett: </strong><em>Does the Internet make all of this possible?</em></p>
<p>It certainly  helps! I&#8217;ve made my living through the internet for 10+ years now. One  of the best features of internet-based businesses is that you can get  instant feedback on the feasibility of your business for a very small  cost (sometimes even free). It also helps to keep expenses low, which is  especially important when you&#8217;re starting without much capital.</p>
<p><strong>Everett: </strong><em>What  are some of the big mistakes that most people make when they launch a Very Small Business?</em></p>
<p><strong>Chris:</strong> Here&#8217;s a short list of big mistakes &#8212; I  think they&#8217;re all important:</p>
<ol>
<li>Failure to consider why  other people should care about what they&#8217;re offering</li>
<li>Failure  to think carefully about how they&#8217;ll actually get paid</li>
<li>Failure  to develop a strategy to market to existing customers (it&#8217;s much easier  to sell to someone who&#8217;s already purchased something)</li>
<li>Poor  follow-through or simply giving up too early</li>
</ol>
<p>If you can  avoid those four mistakes, especially #1, you&#8217;ll be off to a much  better start than a lot of people.</p>
<p><strong>Everett: </strong><em>Is there anyone in  particular who inspires you right  now?</em></p>
<div id=":1j6">
<p><strong>Chris:</strong> There are so many people! These days I am mostly  encouraged by my readers, who regularly write in from all over the  world. A few other people:</p>
<p>I read <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Losing-My-Virginity-Survived-Business/dp/0812932293/ref=sr_1_1?ie=UTF8&amp;s=books&amp;qid=1266796275&amp;sr=8-1">Richard Branson&#8217;s  autobiography</a> on this trip. I&#8217;m not sure why I&#8217;ve never followed him  much before &#8212; he&#8217;s really quite amazing, and definitely a major role  model for unconventional entrepreneurs.</p>
<p>Paul Farmer is the  ultimate social entrepreneur. My friend Scott Harrison is doing a great  job at creating a social movement around addressing the global water  crisis.</p>
<p>And last but not least, I always mention my personal  heroes, Dr. Gary and Susan Parker, who have lived in West Africa for  more than 20 years now. Nothing I do in business or anything else  compares with their great work, but I hope to eventually have at least  10% as much impact on the world as they do.<br/>
<strong><br/>
Everett: </strong><em>You&#8217;ve spoken  about the pressure to hire more people for your business, why did  you decide not to?</em></p>
<p><strong>Chris:</strong> Because I&#8217;m not good at managing people. I  like leadership but not management. I want to create new things instead  of manage existing things &#8212; that&#8217;s where I derive my energy from.  Also, I chose not to hire people simply because it&#8217;s unnecessary. I can  make all the money I need without expanding. I travel to 25 countries a  year and work from everywhere I go. I&#8217;m writing these answers to you  while sitting on the floor in Manila airport, waiting to fly to Papua  New Guinea. I&#8217;d rather be doing this than managing employees, virtual or  otherwise.</p>
<p><strong>Everett:</strong><em> Which of your unconventional products has had the  most success? Why do you think that is?</em></p>
<p><strong>Chris:</strong> Good question. I  was surprised to see that <em>Frequent Flyer Master</em> quickly became  the #1 seller. (Credit where credit is due to <a id="iert" title="Jonathan Fields" href="http://jonathanfields.com/">Jonathan  Fields</a>, who predicted this.) In retrospect I think it was because  the benefit was extremely clear &#8212; buy this product for $49, get at  least 25k miles or enough for a free flight. Easy to understand and  compelling for the right audience.</p>
<p>The <em>$100 Business Forum</em>,  which is more of a community group than a product, also did very well  in selling out in 90 minutes after the launch. We&#8217;re setting up more  groups for later in the year, but I want to be careful we don&#8217;t do too  much too fast with that.</p>
<p><strong> Everett: </strong><em>What goals do you have for your  business in 2010?</em></p>
<p><strong>Chris:</strong> I want to double revenue,  double the active client base, and increase the total product line to  about $1,000 in offerings. The biggest product launch will be the  upcoming <em>Empire Building Kit</em>, where I&#8217;m planning to  reverse-engineer the entire process of creating a lifestyle business at  the $50k-150k / year level. Right now I&#8217;m collecting case studies for  that and outlining screen-capture videos as I travel.</p>
<p>After  those things are done, the second half of the year will focus much more  on my other goals. My first book is coming out in September, and I&#8217;m  going on an <em>Unconventional Book Tour</em> to every state and province  in the U.S. and Canada. Then of course I also have to visit 20+ new  countries to continue making progress on my journey to every country in  the world. All of these projects are fairly intensive, but they&#8217;re also a  lot of fun. Without the business, of course, all of the other things  would be much more difficult.</p>
