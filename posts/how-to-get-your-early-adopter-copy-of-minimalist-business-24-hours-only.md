<p>Everyone has a moment when they start to believe.</p>
<p>This is the moment when it all starts to make sense, when the pieces click into place, and success becomes inevitable.</p>
<p>My moment came in the summer of 2008. I was working full time as a photo editor at <em>New York</em> Magazine.</p>
<p>This was before the near collapse of the banking system, but there was still very little money going around. Especially in the photo industry &#8211;it&#8217;s incredibly difficult to get gigs when there are 3 million photographers and most of them sell their stuff for $1 on istockphoto.</p>
<p>I attended a small convention of photographers in downtown Manhattan with my friend <a href="http://www.dianasabreen.com/">Diana Sabreen</a>, who worked for me as a photographer at the time.</p>
<p>Photography conventions are incredibly sad. There are typically thousands of underpaid and dispirited professional photographers attending &#8212; they&#8217;ve bought all the gear, but they can&#8217;t find the work, and the underlying purpose of conventions is simply to give Nikon and Canon a venue to sell photographers more gear that they don&#8217;t need.</p>
<p><strong>How I discovered that you can live and work from anywhere.</strong></p>
<p>One speaker stood out to me though, Rob Haggart. He claimed to be a photo editor, but wasn&#8217;t working at a magazine anymore. Instead he ran a blog called <a href="http://www.aphotoeditor.com/">A Photo Editor</a>.</p>
<p>All of the photographers loved him. It was impossible to talk with him at the convention because their was such a crowd around this guy. I couldn&#8217;t figure out why, because he apparently didn&#8217;t do anything &#8212; he obviously couldn&#8217;t employ these people because he didn&#8217;t work at a magazine or agency. Why was he so successful?</p>
<p>Later at drinks down on The Bowery in Manhattan I walked up and asked Rob a simple question: &#8220;Photo editor to photo editor, what do you actually do?&#8221;</p>
<p>&#8220;I sell photo portfolio websites, and spend most of my time hiking and raft around Colorado with my kids.&#8221; and then he added quite mysteriously &#8220;<strong>&#8230;It really is possible to live and work from anywhere.</strong>&#8221; Then he walked away.</p>
<p>I had another gin and tonic and headed home, but his words stayed with me until I quit my job in July of 2009 with a single intention: to move across the country and learn how to live and work from anywhere.</p>
<p>What I found during this experiment was stunning, it really is possible to live and work from anywhere.</p>
<p><strong>You can create a minimalist business in order to live and work from anywhere.<br/>
</strong><br/>
The Internet has broken down all of the barriers in communication, and this in turn has broken down all of the barriers in sales. If there are no costs, you can sell to anyone in the world.</p>
<p>Any one person can contribute value to a tribe of 1000, or 10,000 people who will support them online.</p>
<p>Shortly after I announced earlier this year that my e-book <em><a href="http://www.artofbeingminimalist.com/">The Art of Being Minimalist</a></em> was fully supporting my minimalist lifestyle, I started to receive a flood of emails from people asking what I&#8217;d done to be able to live and work from anywhere.</p>
<p>These emails haven&#8217;t stopped. Everyone wants to know how to create a business that allows them to live and work from anywhere in the world. I&#8217;ve done my best to answer every email, but the sheer volume made me realize that I had to record my thoughts in a place where they were more widely available to people who need the knowledge.</p>
<p><strong>I believe this development, how to live and work from anywhere, will be remembered as the single most important change in the history of the modern age.</strong></p>
<p>Slowly, and quite silently, a small legion of extraordinary individuals are learning how to harness the power of the Internet to create location independent micro-businesses which allow them to move when and where they please.</p>
<p><strong>I also believe:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>It&#8217;s a lot easier to start a minimalist business than it is to find a fulfilling job with the current state of the economy.</li>
<li>An MB (minimalist businessman) doesn&#8217;t need to work as many hours as a typical wage slave (I currently work only 10 hours a week, but I&#8217;m angling for 4.)</li>
<li>Anyone with enough strength and willpower now has access to the resources that allow them to create a minimalist business.</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>So why isn&#8217;t everyone creating a minimalist already?</strong></p>
<p>Because of fear. The world changes slower we like to think. Many people want guarantees, they want someone to point them in the right direction and physically show them every single step they should take.</p>
<p>Ironically, doing exactly what everyone else isn&#8217;t doing is the easiest way to find success.</p>
<p>Most people are trained from kindergarten to sit down, shut up, and do as their told. Doing what you&#8217;re told makes it pretty impossible to start a minimalist business, because there&#8217;s no one to tell you what to do. You&#8217;re the only employee!</p>
<p>There are no 12 simple steps to a minimalist business. There are no magic bullets.</p>
<p>I can&#8217;t tell you every single thing you need to do to make a business successful, because every minimalist business relies on the individuality of it&#8217;s creator.</p>
<p>The unique qualities that make you different are what you have to harness to create a minimalist business.</p>
<p>Only a few people on the cutting edge of change will gather the strength to make this journey. Eventually, the rest may follow, but for right now this really is frontier territory.</p>
