<p><a href="/assets/posts/how-to-avoid-scaling-your-life-overhead-with-your-income-img1.jpg"><img class="alignleft" src="/assets/posts/how-to-avoid-scaling-your-life-overhead-with-your-income-img1.jpg" alt="" width="263" height="350"/></a>One of the biggest challenges of minimalism, especially when you apply it to the idea of creating a minimalist business, is avoiding the inevitable pull and pressures to scale up your life expenses with the rise of your personal wealth.</p>
<p>There&#8217;s a good deal of pressure in society to spend more money. We congregate around malls in most parts of the country, there are advertisements to buy buy buy everywhere, there are endless luxuries that we&#8217;re told will make us happier.</p>
<p><strong>Why spending more won&#8217;t make you happier.</strong></p>
<p>Obviously if you&#8217;ve been following my writing for any amount of time, you know that I&#8217;m convinced that buying stuff doesn&#8217;t make you happier &#8212; it just tethers you location and consumes your income.</p>
<p>I originally discovered the idea of minimalism when I left my day job to pursue a location independent life. In order to do that, I had to figure out how to live on very limited resources.</p>
<p>I asked the question: how do I survive without money? Inevitably that lead to minimalism, which lead to living with less than 100 things and being able to live and work from anywhere.</p>
<p>However inevitable it might have been from the beginning, I never conceived of the idea that my income would reach the level that it has in such a short amount of time.</p>
<p><strong>The dangers of income growth.</strong></p>
<p>Once your minimalist business grows (and if you do the right things it will) you might discover the same challenge.</p>
<p>You&#8217;ll suddenly find yourself working less than 10 hours a week, and making more than you did at your day job.</p>
<p>When you don&#8217;t scale your income with your overhead, you suddenly produce a surplus of money which you can use to your advantage &#8212; say to get out of debt, retire early, or simply pursue the dreams that you&#8217;re passionate about.</p>
<p>That&#8217;s why I&#8217;ve prepared this list of 16 strategies to keep your life-overhead from scaling in direct proportion to your income. I hope this list can help you keep your spending low and your income high, whether you&#8217;ve successfully created a minimalist business, or you&#8217;re trying to leave your soul-sucking day job.</p>
<h4>Here are 16 strategies to keep your overhead from scaling with your income:</h4>
<p><strong>1. Use free transportation.</strong></p>
<p>One of the easiest and healthiest ways to keep your overhead low is to use free or inexpensive transportation. We live in a society where having a car is the norm, however cars are expensive, destructive, dirty, and anti-social. If you care about the state of the Gulf oil spill, I&#8217;d better not see you driving. The truth about the matter is that it&#8217;s fairly easy to live car-free by purchasing a bike, walking, or simply using public transportation.</p>
<p><strong>2. Live in a place that&#8217;s walkable. </strong></p>
<p>Not all cities are created equal. Places like Portland, OR. New York, and San Francisco are created in a way that you can obtain everything you need to survive by walking a couple of blocks. If you live in a city or the suburbs where sprawl is the norm, you&#8217;re keeping your overhead high by needing a car to obtain your groceries. Stop, think about where you&#8217;re living, and make the right choice in order to keep your overhead low.</p>
<p><strong>3. Prepare your own food. </strong></p>
<p>Eating out for every meal is costly, and also not healthy. Fast food, and even most restaurant food, is filled with stuff you don&#8217;t even want to know about, especially salt, fat, and processed sugar that metabolizes faster than our bodies can handle. If you prepare your own food out of whole ingredients such as vegetables, meats, beans and grains, you&#8217;ll both lose weight and save money. Shop the periphery of the supermarket, only buy unprocessed food. Jules just came out with a <a href="http://thestonesoup.com/blog/2010/06/a-free-e-cookbook/">free minimalist cookbook</a> that can help you with this.</p>
<p><strong>4. Track your possessions.</strong></p>
<p>Nothing can blow your overhead out of proportion like buying lots of junk you don&#8217;t need. The easiest way to keep your stuff under control is to commit to living with less than 100 personal possessions. I&#8217;ve been doing it long enough now that I wouldn&#8217;t even dream of living any other way, it&#8217;s just not practical to have to worry about lots of stuff everywhere.</p>
<p><strong>5. Live in a smaller space.</strong></p>
<p>One of the big fallacies of the American Dream is the McMansion that MTV convinced us we were supposed to buy. Having a big house with a huge yard and a two-car garage can or will blow your overhead out of proportion. Opt-out of this lie and rent a smaller space in a walkable area.</p>
<p><strong>6. Avoid watching TV.</strong></p>
<p>The television is designed with handy 5-minute breaks to convince you to buy an unrealistic amount of stuff that will quickly swell your overhead. If you ate all of the junk food that comes up in one hour of typical commercial breaks, you&#8217;d die. Avoid this situation by not being a passive consumer of mindless entertainment, destroy your TV and cancel your cable.</p>
<p><strong>7. Avoid reading mass media.</strong></p>
<p>Newspapers and magazines are created around the same advertising model, which is largely unsustainable &#8212; that&#8217;s why the newspaper and magazine industries are dying. If you look at your average fashion magazine, you&#8217;ll be convinced the only way to be cool is to spend $6000 on a handbag. This is absurd, you don&#8217;t five-hundred beauty products and sparkling gold jewels. All of this stuff was created to make other people rich and brain wash you into living a life with no meaning. Don&#8217;t read newspapers or magazines as most of them encourage consumption (and also kill trees.)</p>
<p><strong>8. Establish a minimalist social circle.</strong></p>
<p>Be careful who you hang around with. If your best friend&#8217;s idea of having fun is racking up credit card debt at the mall, you have a social circle problem. Cultivate relationships around less and encourage people you know to embrace minimalism, or find friends who already have. A great way to do this to share minimalist writing through your social networks like Facebook and Twitter in order to make it clear to people where your priorities lie. Invite friends over for dinner and enjoy good conversation over inexpensive home-prepared food instead of going to the movies or spending hundreds of dollars out at the bar.</p>
<p><strong>9. Share resources.</strong></p>
<p>We all done need everything that we&#8217;ve been told we do. Cars for instance are quickly becoming a shared commodity in most cities because of amazing resources like <a href="http://www.zipercar.com/">Zipcar</a>. There are of course countless other ways to share resources. Join a tool lending library for when you need to create things (these exist Portland and Oakland, and if your cities doesn&#8217;t have one you should convince them it&#8217;s necessary.) Use <a href="http://us.zilok.com/">Zikol</a> to rent anything that you need ,or offer your own useful items for rent in your neighborhood. Consider setting up small neighborhood collectives to share things that you might not need on a regular basis. This is becoming easier with social networking and the rise of the Internet.</p>
<p><strong>10. Pursue simple pleasures.</strong></p>
<p>The idea that you have to spend money to be happy is absurd. Realize that simple things such as sitting at the beach, or on a bench at the park can be a free or inexpensive way to spend time. Cooking food can be a great way to get enjoyment and also pass the time. Read books about things that matter in order to improve your knowledge of the world and pass time. Lately I&#8217;ve been volunteering to crew sailboats on San Francisco bay, which is a free and helpful way to have an amazing day.</p>
<p><strong>11. Use simple tools.</strong></p>
<p>There are so many expensive gadgets and tools out there to buy. The pressure to upgrade to the latest and greatest nonsense is absurd. You don&#8217;t need five different ways to access the Internet, you only need one. You don&#8217;t need to invest in the top of the line gadget when you only need a simple tool to get the job done. Sometimes a simple pad of paper is the best way to get any job done.</p>
<p><strong>12. Do less.</strong></p>
<p>Walk slower, breathe oxygen, simply be content sitting and watching the trees sway back and forth. All of the endless and frantic running around won&#8217;t be remembered, it will just make you tired. When you slow down and do less, you begin to realize that everyone is doing way too much. Why work 60 hours a week when you can work 10? Why run to the grocery store when you can walk slowly? Walk slowly, breathe, do less.</p>
<p><strong>13. Focus on the work that matters.</strong></p>
<p>Not all work is created equal. A large number of people I know are caught up in routines that just spend lots of time, but aren&#8217;t creating any value. When you spend your time creating things that help people, and automating your distribution process, you can eventually spend a lot less time working and a lot more time enjoying your minimalist life. Eliminate all activities that aren&#8217;t creating value for you, or anyone else, and focus on the important.</p>
<p><strong>14. Dedicate time to self-education over all else.</strong></p>
<p>We&#8217;re taught that we need to be taught to learn things, I&#8217;ve found that the opposite is true. Self-education can be the most effective way to use your time. There are hundreds of free, or inexpensive resources that can help you learn a huge amount of information. If you&#8217;re wondering what to do with your life, don&#8217;t go buy a pizza and play video games. Instead, log on to <a href="http://www.ted.com/">TED</a> and watch some of the world&#8217;s greatest minds talk about the ideas that they&#8217;re passionate about. Don&#8217;t spend $150,000 on a business degree when <a href="http://unconventionalguides.com/cmd.php?Clk=3720851">Empire Builder</a> or a <a href="http://personalmba.com/">Personal MBA</a> can give you the tools to create a very small business for a small fraction of that price. Resolve to read a book a week for the rest of your life &#8212; believe it or not simply reading give you the keys to creating your ideal reality.</p>
<p><strong>15. Realize that you already have more than enough.</strong></p>
<p>We&#8217;ve been living with so much more than we ever needed for generations. When you wake up and realize that advertising tricked you into consuming so much more than you ever needed, and that you can be content right here and now, you suddenly have the key to keeping your overhead low in order to prevent your life from scaling with your income. You don&#8217;t need anything else, everything you have now is enough.</p>
<p><strong>16. Keep the end goal in mind.</strong></p>
<p>The end is the beginning is the end. Don&#8217;t get distracted by meaningless pursuits by setting an end goal that has some meaning to you. Do you want to leave your soul-sucking day job in order to pursue a minimalist life and live and work from anywhere? Maybe you want to build a boat and sail all around the world? Maybe you just want to sit on your porch and read a good book.</p>
<p>There&#8217;s no reason that your end goal has to scale with your income. If it doesn&#8217;t scale, your income will skyrocket with no-relation to your spending, and freedom can become an inevitability.</p>
