# Bogbook Publishing Schedule

## Posting model: continuous and reactive

Bogbook posts when something worth posting happens. That is the model. There is no "one post per day" or "one post per beat slot." When a story breaks, the researcher surfaces it, the writer posts it within the hour. Then something else breaks and it happens again.

The historical Gawker contract required **12 posts per editor per day, with 8 up before noon**. With two editors, that was 24 posts per day. The target for Bogbook is not 24 — it's as many as the news day justifies, with a floor of **8 items per weekday**. A short item can be drafted and published in 20 minutes. That is the operating assumption.

The beat rotation below is not a posting schedule. It is a map of what to look for. On a slow news day it tells the researcher where to dig. On a busy day the news sets the agenda and the beat rotation keeps the site from accidentally covering only one topic.

Weekends: post if something breaks. Otherwise dark.

## Weekly beat rotation

Each weekday owns a primary beat. The beat anchors the week's editorial thinking and prevents the site from drifting into pure-reaction mode. Breaking news overrides the schedule; the beat is the fallback when nothing is on fire.

### Monday — Silicon Valley Accountability

The week opens with the hardest punch. Founders, VCs, funds, and the capital networks behind them.

Recurring story types:
- The launch that didn't survive contact with reality (what the deck said vs. what shipped)
- The VC conflict of interest the trade press filed away quietly
- The SEC filing nobody else read
- The hagiographic profile and what it left out
- The fund with a checkered LP list

Historical Gawker equivalent: Valleywag Monday drops. The story that set the week's Silicon Valley conversation.

### Tuesday — AI Industry Beat

The biggest ongoing story in tech is the gap between what AI labs announce and what their products do. Tuesday owns it.

Recurring story types:
- Lab announcement vs. six-week reality check
- Benchmark theater (what the eval tests, what the eval misses)
- The AI press release that deserves a fact-check
- The model card vs. the actual product behavior
- AI safety theater: what the "responsible AI" teams actually prevented this quarter
- AI labor displacement: specific companies, specific numbers, not vibes
- The AI startup that raised $X million and shipped a wrapper

This beat did not exist during Gawker's run. It is Bogbook's most differentiated slot. Own it harder than anyone else.

### Wednesday — Media and Press Beat

Who got hired, fired, sold, sued. How the press covers (or doesn't cover) the stories on Monday and Tuesday. The access journalism that protects tech subjects.

Recurring story types:
- The publication that killed a story because a source called in a favor
- The profile subject who is also a major advertiser
- The editor departure that wasn't about "pursuing other opportunities"
- The AI-journalism experiment and its actual traffic numbers
- The newsletter that raised $X million and what the open rates look like now
- The trade press outlet that ran a founder's press release with a byline on it

Historical Gawker equivalent: the media column. Gawker covered its own industry with more ruthlessness than any other beat. Bogbook should do the same, with a tighter focus on how media capture enables Silicon Valley stories to go unreported.

### Thursday — Labor and Politics

Big Tech's relationship with its workforce and its government. The libertarian-to-reactionary pipeline. The Thiel-adjacent network. Policy capture.

Recurring story types:
- The RTO mandate and the real estate deal behind it
- Layoff math: headcount reduction vs. stock buyback vs. executive comp the same quarter
- H-1B as leverage, not as talent pipeline
- Contractor classification and the two-tier workforce
- The tech executive turned policy influencer and who is paying for the influence
- The think tank, the PAC, the foundation, and what they want from Congress

Historical Gawker equivalent: Gawker's politics coverage intersected with power and money, not ideology. Bogbook follows the same frame: this is not left vs. right, it is who is paying whom for what.

### Friday — Scene and Culture

Lighter in tone, still pointed. NYC-centric. Tech money meeting culture. The party, the opening, the real estate transaction, the dinner reservation as social signal.

Recurring story types:
- The conference that nobody will admit was bad
- The book deal that reveals something about who gets to tell the tech story
- The NYC real estate move that tracks where tech money is landing
- The cultural institution that took a donation from someone who shouldn't have given it
- The trend piece about tech workers that required zero actual tech workers as sources
- The "founder lifestyle" feature and its unstated production budget

This is the slot for camp, for the eulogy register, for the list of dead magazines and failed apps. Friday earns the pageantry because Monday through Thursday did the work.

## Recurring post formats

Beyond the beat rotation, several recurring formats run on a flexible schedule (monthly, quarterly, or when the news hook materializes).

### The Reality Check

Format: take a claim made 90 days ago — a product launch, a funding announcement, a hiring plan, an AI milestone — and measure it against what actually happened. No pundit framing, just the numbers.

Frequency: 2–4 per month. Run one whenever a high-profile claim has had enough time to age badly.

### The Press Release Translation

Format: take an actual press release or announcement post and annotate it line by line with what the sentence means in plain English. Keep the PR language in block quotes; the translation goes beneath.

Frequency: as needed. Best when the PR is unusually egregious.

### The Week in VC Theater

Format: brief weekly roundup of funding announcements, with a one-sentence verdict on each. Which rounds are real? Which are bridge rounds dressed as Series A? Which valuations are math and which are vibes?

Frequency: weekly or biweekly. The roundup format earns its keep as a traffic anchor — readers bookmark it.

### The Profile Autopsy

Format: take a recent hagiographic founder or executive profile from a major publication and perform a close read. What did the reporter not ask? What did the subject say that the reporter did not notice was newsworthy? What is the publication's relationship with the subject's company?

Frequency: monthly, triggered by a profile that merits it.

### The Body Count

Format: quarterly tally of layoffs, studio closures, product shutdowns, and pivots at major tech companies. Presented as a list with dates and stated reasons next to what the actual reason appeared to be.

Frequency: quarterly.

## Topics for the AI and tech beat specifically

These are the standing story angles that apply to the Tuesday slot (and bleed into Monday and Thursday). Keep this list current. When a story breaks, find its slot in this taxonomy before deciding how to play it.

**The hype-to-reality gap**
- Benchmark claims vs. live product behavior
- "We are six months from AGI" and the filing cabinet of similar claims
- The capability demo that requires specific conditions not present in the actual product
- The alignment/safety claim vs. the actual deployed system

**The economics of AI**
- GPU burn rate vs. revenue
- The enterprise customer who is "experimenting" and the one who is actually paying
- The API pricing change and what it means for the startups built on it
- The AI wrapper startup that raised pre-product on a demo and a vibe

**The AI labor story**
- Jobs eliminated (with actual numbers, not euphemisms)
- The "AI will create more jobs than it destroys" claim and who is making it this week
- The contractor classification of AI trainers, raters, and RLHF workers
- The outsourced annotation workforce and its working conditions

**The AI press**
- The outlet that covered an AI lab's announcement without mentioning the lab's investor ties to the publication's parent company
- The journalist who became an "AI fellow" at the lab they previously covered
- The AI PR that got published verbatim with a dateline
- The benchmark story that cited the lab's own eval

**The regulatory capture story**
- The AI safety framework that the regulated companies wrote
- The "voluntary commitment" and its enforcement mechanism (there is none)
- The former regulator now at the AI company they were supposed to regulate
- The lobbying spend vs. the stated commitment to responsible deployment

**The AI culture story (for Friday)**
- The AI startup office that costs more per square foot than a hospital
- The executive who talks about "building for humanity" and his security detail
- The AI conference speaker list and its sponsor list as the same list
- The "democratizing AI" claim made by a company that charges $200/month

## Launch sequence

Before the site opens to search traffic (noindex removal), publish in this order:

1. **Inaugural post** — the mission statement as a piece of journalism, not a press release. What Bogbook is, who it is for, why now, and what the Thiel/Gawker backstory means for this site's existence. 800–1,000 words. Camp eulogy register opening into strategist's autopsy.

2. **First accountability piece** — a Monday-beat story already in the news cycle. Demonstrates the site can report, not just editorialize. Named subject, named numbers, named document if possible.

3. **First AI beat piece** — a Tuesday-beat story on the hype-to-reality gap. The AI story that the trade press got wrong or didn't cover hard enough.

After these three are published and verified in production, remove noindex and distribute.

## What Gawker covered that Bogbook will not cover

Old Gawker spent significant space on:
- Celebrity tabloid content (Bogbook has no celebrity beat)
- Gawker Media internal drama (Bogbook has no staff to have drama about)
- Sex tapes and privacy-invasive content (this is the literal line that killed Gawker)
- General pop-culture snark without a power-and-money angle

Bogbook's scope is narrower and harder. Everything on the schedule above connects to who has power, how they got it, what they are doing with it, and what the press is not telling you about it. Lifestyle, celebrity, and pure entertainment content do not belong here regardless of the traffic they would generate.
