OpenAI this week closed a $122 billion funding round at an $852 billion post-money valuation. CFO Sarah Friar announced the company is planning to reserve IPO shares for retail investors, targeting a public listing in the second half of 2026. The coverage was glowing. The number was big. Nobody asked the obvious question.

OpenAI was founded as a nonprofit. Its pitch to early donors — including Elon Musk, who gave $100 million before suing to get it back — was that it would develop artificial general intelligence for the benefit of humanity rather than shareholders. The nonprofit structure was the guarantee that the mission wouldn't get eaten by the returns.

$852 billion is more than Berkshire Hathaway. It is more than Walmart. It is, by a wide margin, the highest valuation ever assigned to a company that began by asking philanthropists to trust it with the future of intelligence.

OpenAI restructured away from its nonprofit governance last year. The move was contested in court and by former employees. The $852 billion round is what that restructuring was for.

The benefit of humanity will be distributed to retail investors at IPO. Sam Altman's cut is reportedly 7%.
