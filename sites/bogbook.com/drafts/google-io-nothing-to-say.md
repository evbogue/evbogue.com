Google I/O 2026 wrapped its keynotes on May 19 and 20. All 85-plus sessions went on-demand today. Apple Insider's headline on the event: "Google I/O 2026 had nothing to say and said it badly, ahead of Apple's WWDC."

The headline is harsher than the reality but not wrong. Google announced Gemini 3.5 Flash — four times faster than comparable models, priced at $1.50 per million input tokens and $9 per million output tokens. It announced Gemini Spark, a personal AI agent with Workspace integration. It announced Docs Live voice editing. It announced Antigravity agent platform upgrades.

None of these are bad. Several are useful. None of them are the thing people were hoping Google would announce, which is evidence that Google has figured out how to be Google again after three years of watching OpenAI eat its lunch.

Gemini 3.5 Flash is competitively priced. It is not a moment. The $1.50 input rate is a pricing decision made in response to pressure, not a product vision. Google has the infrastructure, the data, the research talent, and the distribution to win the AI era. The question I/O 2026 did not answer is whether it has the will to use any of it.

The sessions are on-demand now if you want to look for evidence I missed.
